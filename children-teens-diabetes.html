Children & Teens with Diabetes

Managing Type 1 and Type 2 diabetes in young people

Content last reviewed: January 2026 | AI-assisted content

👶 Type 1 in Children

Type 1 diabetes is the most common form of diabetes in children, accounting for approximately 85-90% of childhood diabetes cases. Unlike Type 2, it is an autoimmune condition where the body's immune system attacks the insulin-producing beta cells in the pancreas.

Characteristics of Childhood Type 1

  • Sudden onset: Symptoms often appear rapidly over days to weeks — unlike the gradual progression seen in Type 2
  • Peak diagnosis ages: 4-6 years and 10-14 years (around puberty)
  • No prevention: Currently cannot be prevented through lifestyle changes
  • Lifelong insulin dependence: Requires insulin from day one of diagnosis
  • DKA risk: Diabetic ketoacidosis may be the first presentation — a medical emergency

Warning Signs in Children

  • Excessive thirst and frequent urination (including bedwetting in previously dry children)
  • Unexplained weight loss despite increased appetite
  • Extreme fatigue and irritability
  • Fruity-smelling breath
  • Blurred vision
  • Nausea, vomiting, and abdominal pain (signs of DKA)
Emergency Warning: If a child shows signs of DKA (vomiting, rapid breathing, confusion, fruity breath), seek emergency care immediately. DKA can be life-threatening and requires IV fluids and insulin in a hospital setting.

📈 Rising Type 2 in Youth

Type 2 diabetes, once called "adult-onset diabetes," is increasingly diagnosed in children and adolescents. This alarming trend is linked to rising rates of childhood obesity and sedentary lifestyles.

Risk Factors for Youth Type 2

  • Obesity: The strongest risk factor — 85% of youth with T2D are overweight or obese
  • Family history: 75-90% have a first or second-degree relative with T2D
  • Ethnicity: Higher rates in certain populations (South Asian, African, Hispanic, Indigenous)
  • Puberty: Insulin resistance naturally increases during puberty
  • Sedentary lifestyle: Excessive screen time, limited physical activity
  • Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS): In adolescent girls
  • Acanthosis nigricans: Dark, velvety skin patches indicating insulin resistance

Key Differences: T2D in Youth vs. Adults

  • Beta cell function declines faster in youth — more aggressive disease progression
  • Complications develop earlier and more rapidly
  • May require insulin sooner than adult-onset T2D
  • Metformin and lifestyle changes are first-line treatment
Prevention Focus: Unlike Type 1, Type 2 diabetes in youth is largely preventable through maintaining healthy weight, regular physical activity, and balanced nutrition. Family involvement is crucial for lasting lifestyle changes.

Blood sugar targets for children and teens vary by age group, reflecting different physiological needs, hypoglycemia risks, and developmental stages. These targets have evolved to be less stringent for younger children.

💉 Insulin Management for Kids

Children with Type 1 diabetes require lifelong insulin therapy. The choice between multiple daily injections (MDI) and insulin pumps depends on the child's age, family preferences, and lifestyle.

Insulin Pumps vs. Multiple Daily Injections

FactorInsulin PumpMDI (Injections)
FlexibilityHigh — easy to adjust basal rates and give small bolusesModerate — set doses, harder to micro-adjust
PrecisionCan deliver very small doses (0.025-0.05 units)Minimum 0.5-1 unit increments with most pens
Best forYoung children, active teens, irregular schedulesTeens preferring fewer devices, those not ready for pump
ChallengesSite changes, device attachment, costMultiple daily injections, less flexibility

Special Considerations for Pediatric Insulin Dosing

  • Honeymoon period: After diagnosis, some beta cell function may remain temporarily, requiring less insulin
  • Growth spurts: Insulin needs increase during growth — monitor and adjust frequently
  • Puberty: Hormones cause significant insulin resistance — doses may need to double
  • Small doses: Young children need tiny, precise doses — pumps and diluted insulin can help
  • Unpredictable eating: Toddlers may not finish meals — consider giving insulin after eating or using extended boluses

Hybrid Closed-Loop Systems

Automated insulin delivery (AID) systems combine an insulin pump with a CGM to automatically adjust basal insulin. These systems significantly improve time in range and reduce hypoglycemia, and are increasingly approved for pediatric use (some from age 2+).

Technology Improves Outcomes: Studies show that children using CGMs and insulin pumps (especially closed-loop systems) have better HbA1c levels, more time in range, and fewer hypoglycemic episodes compared to traditional management.

🍎 School & Diabetes

Children spend a significant portion of their day at school. Proper planning and communication ensure they can participate fully while staying safe.

504 Plans and IEPs (for US/similar systems)

  • Section 504 Plan: A legal document ensuring accommodations for students with diabetes, including:
    • Permission to check blood sugar and administer insulin anywhere, anytime
    • Access to fast-acting glucose for lows
    • Bathroom breaks as needed
    • Ability to eat snacks in class
    • Make-up time for missed work due to diabetes care
    • Trained personnel to assist with diabetes management
  • Individualized Healthcare Plan (IHP): Outlines specific medical needs and emergency procedures

Communicating with School Staff

  • Meet with teachers, school nurse, PE teachers, and administrators before school starts
  • Provide written instructions for hypoglycemia and hyperglycemia treatment
  • Train multiple staff members to recognize and treat lows
  • Ensure the child is never left unsupervised during a low
  • Discuss field trip and activity accommodations

Handling Lows at School

  • Keep fast-acting glucose in multiple locations (classroom, nurse's office, gym, cafeteria)
  • The child should never be sent to the nurse alone during a suspected low
  • Glucagon should be available and staff trained to use it
  • CGM alerts can be shared with school nurse's phone (with parental consent)
Never Delay Treatment: A child experiencing hypoglycemia should be treated immediately — wherever they are. Never make a child walk to the nurse's office during a low, and never leave them alone.

🏃 Sports & Physical Activity

Physical activity is essential for all children, including those with diabetes. With proper planning, children with diabetes can participate in any sport or activity.

Benefits of Exercise

  • Improves insulin sensitivity and blood sugar control
  • Supports healthy weight and cardiovascular health
  • Boosts mood and reduces stress
  • Builds confidence and social connections

Adjusting Insulin for Activity

  • Reduce bolus insulin: Consider 25-75% reduction for meals before activity
  • Reduce basal insulin: Pump users can set temporary basal rates (50-80%) 1-2 hours before exercise
  • Different sports, different effects: Aerobic activity lowers glucose; intense anaerobic activity (sprinting, competition stress) may raise it temporarily
  • Timing matters: Exercise shortly after meals may reduce post-meal spikes; exercise during fasting increases hypo risk
Coach and Team Communication: Inform coaches and teammates about diabetes. Ensure the coach knows the signs of hypoglycemia, keeps fast-acting glucose on the sideline, and understands the child may need breaks to check blood sugar or treat lows.

🧠 Emotional & Social Challenges

Living with diabetes during childhood and adolescence presents unique emotional and social challenges. Mental health is just as important as physical health in diabetes management.

Diabetes Burnout

Diabetes is relentless — it requires attention 24/7, 365 days a year. Burnout is common, especially in teens:

  • Feeling overwhelmed by constant management demands
  • Skipping blood sugar checks or insulin doses
  • Frustration when efforts don't produce expected results
  • "Taking a break" from diabetes care

Peer Pressure and Fitting In

  • Desire to be "normal" and not stand out
  • Embarrassment about checking blood sugar or injecting in public
  • Pressure to eat what friends eat at parties or gatherings
  • Questions from peers that feel intrusive or annoying

Body Image Concerns

  • Weight gain from insulin (especially during improved control)
  • Visible devices (pumps, CGMs) affecting body image
  • Risk of "diabulimia" — intentionally reducing insulin to lose weight (dangerous and potentially fatal)

Depression and Anxiety

Youth with diabetes have 2-3 times higher rates of depression and anxiety. Warning signs include:

  • Withdrawal from friends and activities
  • Declining grades or school attendance
  • Changes in sleep or appetite
  • Loss of interest in diabetes management
  • Expressing hopelessness or worthlessness
Mental Health Screening: Regular mental health screening should be part of diabetes care. If your child shows signs of depression, anxiety, or disordered eating, seek help from a mental health professional experienced in chronic illness.

👨‍👩‍👧 Family Involvement

Diabetes affects the entire family. Successful management requires teamwork while gradually building the child's independence.

Parent and Caregiver Roles

  • Toddlers/preschoolers: Parents handle all aspects of diabetes care
  • School-age: Gradual introduction of self-care tasks under supervision
  • Teens: Increasing independence with parental oversight and support
  • Avoid the "diabetes police" approach — nagging and criticism backfire
  • Use collaborative problem-solving, not blame

Impact on Siblings

  • Siblings may feel neglected when diabetes requires parental attention
  • They may worry about their sibling's health or fear developing diabetes themselves
  • Some siblings take on caregiving roles inappropriately
  • Others may feel jealous of "special treatment" (extra snacks, attention)

Balancing Independence and Safety

  • Gradually transfer responsibility based on maturity, not just age
  • Use technology (CGM sharing) to monitor while giving space
  • Establish check-in routines rather than constant oversight
  • Allow natural consequences for minor lapses (within safe limits)
  • Celebrate successes and progress in self-management
Family Therapy: If diabetes is causing significant family conflict or stress, consider family therapy with a counselor experienced in chronic illness. It can improve communication, reduce conflict, and support better diabetes outcomes.

🎂 Birthdays, Parties & Social Events

Children with diabetes should enjoy celebrations just like their peers. With planning, birthday cake and party foods can be part of their lives.

Carb Counting for Treats

  • Learn to estimate carbs in common party foods (cake, pizza, ice cream)
  • Use phone apps to look up carb counts for various foods
  • Pre-bolus before eating high-carb/high-fat foods when possible
  • Consider extended boluses (for pumps) for pizza and other fatty foods

Party Planning Tips

  • Communicate with party hosts about food being served
  • Offer to bring a diabetes-friendly dish or treat
  • Have your child eat a balanced meal before parties with unpredictable food
  • Focus on the social experience, not just the food
  • Don't make diabetes an excuse to skip events

Not Feeling Left Out

  • Children with diabetes can eat the same foods as others — they just need insulin for carbs
  • Avoid labels like "good foods" and "bad foods"
  • Teach your child to advocate for themselves at events
  • Help them practice responses to questions or comments about their food choices
Moderation, Not Deprivation: Strict food restrictions can lead to secretive eating, guilt, and disordered eating patterns. Teaching carb counting and insulin matching allows for flexibility and a healthy relationship with food.

🔄 Transitioning to Adult Care

The transition from pediatric to adult diabetes care is a critical period. Poor transitions can lead to gaps in care and worse outcomes. Planning should begin in early adolescence.

When to Transition

  • Typically between ages 18-25, depending on the healthcare system
  • Transition is a process, not a single event — should take 1-2 years minimum
  • Readiness should be assessed based on self-management skills, not just age

Preparing for Transition

Age RangeSkills to Develop
11-13 yearsBasic understanding of diabetes, recognizing highs/lows, beginning to track numbers
14-16 yearsIndependent blood sugar checks, carb counting, understanding medications, communicating with healthcare providers
17-18 yearsManaging prescriptions, scheduling appointments, understanding insurance/healthcare system, knowing when to seek help
18+ yearsFull self-management, navigating adult healthcare, managing diabetes during major life transitions (college, work, living independently)

The Transition Process

  • Pediatric team should introduce the concept of transition early (age 12-14)
  • Gradual increase in independent clinic visits (without parents in the room)
  • Transfer of medical records and summary to the adult provider
  • Ideally, a joint meeting or warm handoff between pediatric and adult teams
  • Follow-up to ensure the young adult has connected with adult care

Challenges During Transition

  • Loss of long-term relationships with pediatric providers
  • Different approach in adult care (more self-directed, less family involvement)
  • Competing priorities (college, jobs, relationships)
  • Gaps in health insurance coverage
  • Higher rates of missed appointments and lost to follow-up
Critical Period: The years surrounding transition (18-25) show the highest rates of poor glycemic control and complications development. Ensure a structured transition plan and continued support during this vulnerable time.
