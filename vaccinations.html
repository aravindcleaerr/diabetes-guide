Vaccinations for Diabetics

Why immunisation is especially important when you live with diabetes

Content last reviewed: January 2026 | AI-assisted content

🛡️ Why Vaccinations Matter More for Diabetics

Diabetes — particularly when blood glucose levels are poorly controlled — can weaken the immune system, making you more susceptible to infections and more likely to develop serious complications from common illnesses. Vaccinations are one of the simplest and most effective ways to protect yourself.

Immune response to vaccines may be somewhat reduced in people with diabetes, especially if HbA1c is elevated. This makes timely vaccination even more important — do not skip or delay recommended shots.

💉 Recommended Vaccines

1. Influenza (Flu) Vaccine

  • Frequency: Every year (annually), ideally before the start of flu season
  • Why: People with diabetes are three times more likely to be hospitalised from flu complications
  • Type: Inactivated influenza vaccine (injection); avoid the live nasal spray if immunocompromised
  • Best time: September–October in the Northern Hemisphere; before monsoon season in India

2. Pneumococcal Vaccine

  • Vaccines: PCV15 or PCV20 (conjugate), followed by PPSV23 (polysaccharide) if PCV15 was given
  • Frequency: At least one dose for all adults with diabetes; a booster of PPSV23 after age 65 if first dose was given earlier
  • Why: Pneumococcal pneumonia, bacteraemia, and meningitis are more common and more severe in diabetics

3. Hepatitis B Vaccine

  • Frequency: 2- or 3-dose series (depending on formulation) for all unvaccinated adults with diabetes aged 19–59; recommended for ages 60+ based on risk
  • Why: Diabetics are at increased risk due to blood glucose monitoring, shared equipment in care settings, and potential for assisted monitoring
  • Note: Heplisav-B (2-dose series) or Engerix-B / Recombivax HB (3-dose series)

4. COVID-19 Vaccine

  • Frequency: Primary series plus recommended boosters as per current guidelines
  • Why: Diabetes is a major risk factor for severe COVID-19, ICU admission, and death
  • Special note: Blood sugar may temporarily rise after vaccination — monitor more frequently for 48–72 hours post-jab

5. Tdap (Tetanus, Diphtheria, Pertussis)

  • Frequency: One dose of Tdap if not previously received, then Td booster every 10 years
  • Why: Diabetics with foot ulcers or wounds are at increased tetanus risk; pertussis protection is important for those around infants

6. Shingles (Zoster) Vaccine — Shingrix

  • Frequency: Two doses, 2–6 months apart, for adults aged 50 and older
  • Why: Diabetes increases the risk of herpes zoster (shingles) and post-herpetic neuralgia
  • Type: Shingrix (recombinant, adjuvanted) — preferred over the older live vaccine Zostavax

❓ Special Considerations for Diabetics

  • Blood sugar monitoring: Check glucose more frequently for 2–3 days after any vaccination, as the immune response can temporarily raise blood sugar
  • Injection site: Rotate injection sites; avoid areas used for insulin injection if possible to reduce confusion about local reactions
  • Sick-day rules apply: If you develop mild fever or malaise post-vaccination, follow your sick-day management plan
  • Immunocompromised patients: Those on immunosuppressive medications (e.g., post-transplant) should avoid live vaccines and consult their care team
  • Travel vaccines: If travelling, consult a travel medicine specialist — additional vaccines (Hepatitis A, typhoid, yellow fever) may be recommended
Keep a vaccination record in your diabetes folder. Share it with your endocrinologist at every visit so they can flag any gaps.

🧬 Reduced Immune Response

Studies show that people with diabetes — especially those with chronically elevated blood glucose — may mount a weaker antibody response to vaccines. This does not mean vaccines are ineffective; they still provide significant protection. Key points:

  • Good glycaemic control before vaccination may improve immune response
  • Even a partial immune response is far better than no protection at all
  • Booster doses (where recommended) help compensate for any reduced initial response
  • Discuss antibody testing with your doctor if you have concerns (e.g., Hepatitis B surface antibody post-vaccination)
