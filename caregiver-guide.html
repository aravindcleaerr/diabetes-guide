Caregiver Guide

How to support a loved one with diabetes without burning out

💭 Understanding the Daily Burden

Living with diabetes means making dozens of health-related decisions every single day — what to eat, when to test, how much insulin to take, whether to exercise, how to handle a sick day. This relentless decision-making is called "diabetes distress" and affects up to 45% of people with diabetes. As a caregiver, understanding this burden is the first step to being genuinely helpful.

  • Diabetes management never takes a day off — there are no weekends or holidays
  • Even with perfect adherence, blood sugar can be unpredictable due to stress, illness, hormones, and dozens of other factors
  • The emotional toll is real: fear of complications, guilt about "bad" numbers, frustration with the healthcare system
  • Your loved one may not always want to talk about their diabetes — and that is okay

🤝 How to Support Without Nagging

The line between support and nagging is thin. Here are practical guidelines:

  • Do: Ask "How can I help?" rather than "Did you check your sugar?"
  • Do: Make healthy meals for the whole family — not separate "diabetic food"
  • Do: Offer to exercise together — walk after dinner, join a yoga class
  • Do: Learn about diabetes so you understand what they are dealing with
  • Don't: Police their food choices or comment on what they are eating
  • Don't: React with alarm or anger to a high glucose reading
  • Don't: Say things like "Should you be eating that?" or "My aunt cured her diabetes with..."
  • Don't: Take over their management unless they ask for help
The golden rule: Support their autonomy. Your role is to be a partner, not a manager.

Daily Support Routine

Morning
Help prepare a balanced breakfast; remind about morning medications if asked
Midday
Offer to walk together after lunch; keep healthy snacks accessible
Afternoon
Be available for emotional check-in; help with appointment scheduling
Evening
Cook a diabetes-friendly dinner for the whole family; exercise together
Night
Ensure supplies are stocked for the next day; know where the emergency kit is

⚠️ Recognising Hypoglycaemia and Hyperglycaemia

Signs of Low Blood Sugar (Hypoglycaemia — below 70 mg/dL)

  • Shakiness, trembling hands
  • Sweating (cold, clammy skin)
  • Rapid heartbeat
  • Confusion, difficulty speaking, irritability
  • Dizziness or lightheadedness
  • In severe cases: seizures, loss of consciousness

What to do: Give 15 g of fast-acting glucose (3–4 glucose tablets, half a glass of juice or regular soda, 1 tablespoon of sugar/honey). Wait 15 minutes, recheck. If still low, repeat. If unconscious, do NOT put anything in their mouth — use glucagon injection and call emergency services.

Signs of High Blood Sugar (Hyperglycaemia — above 250 mg/dL)

  • Excessive thirst and dry mouth
  • Frequent urination
  • Fatigue and lethargy
  • Blurred vision
  • Nausea (may indicate DKA — a medical emergency)
  • Fruity-smelling breath (DKA sign)
If your loved one has blood sugar above 300 mg/dL with nausea, vomiting, or fruity breath, seek emergency medical care immediately. This could be diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA).

🛑 Emergency Preparedness for Caregivers

  • Know where the glucose tablets, glucagon kit, and diabetes emergency kit are stored
  • Learn how to use glucagon — practise with an expired kit
  • Keep the endocrinologist's phone number and nearest hospital emergency number saved in your phone
  • Know the sick-day management rules
  • Ensure your loved one wears a medical ID bracelet or carries a medical card

💚 Emotional Support

  • Acknowledge that diabetes is hard — validation goes a long way
  • Listen without trying to fix everything
  • Celebrate small wins: a good HbA1c result, consistent exercise, a week of regular testing
  • Watch for signs of depression or diabetes burnout: withdrawal, skipping medications, not checking glucose, expressing hopelessness
  • Encourage professional help if needed — therapy, diabetes support groups, or a diabetes educator
  • Remember that a "bad" glucose number is not a moral failing — it is just data

🛠️ Practical Help

  • Meal preparation: Cook diabetes-friendly meals for everyone; stock healthy snacks; learn to read nutrition labels
  • Appointment accompaniment: Offer to go to doctor visits — a second pair of ears helps catch information
  • Medication management: Help organise medications, set reminders, pick up prescriptions
  • Insurance and paperwork: Assist with insurance claims, medication authorisation, and medical records
  • Technology: Help set up glucose meters, apps, or CGM devices if your loved one needs assistance

🔥 Caregiver Burnout

Taking care of someone with a chronic condition is exhausting. Caregiver burnout is real and common. Signs include:

  • Feeling constantly tired or overwhelmed
  • Resentment or anger toward your loved one
  • Neglecting your own health
  • Social withdrawal
  • Sleep problems

What to do:

  • Set boundaries — you cannot manage their diabetes for them
  • Take breaks and maintain your own hobbies and social life
  • Share caregiving responsibilities with other family members
  • Join a caregiver support group (online or in-person)
  • Consider counselling for yourself
  • Remember: you cannot pour from an empty cup
