Self-monitoring techniques, continuous glucose monitors, target ranges, and pattern recognition

🎯 Why Monitor Blood Sugar?

Blood glucose monitoring is the cornerstone of day-to-day diabetes management. It allows individuals with diabetes to:

  • Understand how food, exercise, stress, illness, and medications affect their glucose levels
  • Make informed decisions about insulin doses, food intake, and activity
  • Detect and treat hypoglycemia (low blood sugar) and hyperglycemia (high blood sugar) early
  • Identify patterns and trends that guide medication adjustments
  • Provide data for healthcare providers to optimize treatment plans
  • Reduce the risk of long-term complications through tighter glycemic control

🩸 Self-Monitoring of Blood Glucose (SMBG)

What Is SMBG?

SMBG involves using a portable glucometer (blood glucose meter) to measure blood glucose from a small drop of capillary blood, usually obtained by pricking a fingertip with a lancet device.

How to Use a Glucometer — Step by Step

  1. Wash your hands thoroughly with warm water and soap. Dry completely. Food residue or moisture on fingers can give falsely high or low readings. Avoid alcohol swabs (they can interfere with some strips).
  2. Prepare the lancet device: Insert a fresh lancet and set the depth to the lowest comfortable setting. Adjust depth upward if you do not get enough blood.
  3. Insert a test strip into the glucometer. The meter will turn on automatically and display that it is ready.
  4. Lance the side of your fingertip: The sides are less painful than the pad. Rotate fingers to avoid building calluses on any one spot.
  5. Obtain a blood drop: Gently squeeze the finger from the base toward the tip. Do not squeeze too hard, as this can dilute the sample with interstitial fluid.
  6. Apply blood to the test strip: Touch the blood drop to the edge of the strip. The strip will wick the blood in by capillary action. Ensure the strip is fully filled.
  7. Wait for the result: Most modern meters display results in 3–5 seconds.
  8. Record the result: Log the reading along with the date, time, and context (fasting, pre-meal, post-meal, bedtime, before/after exercise, during illness).
  9. Dispose of the lancet safely in a sharps container. Used test strips can go in regular trash.
Tips for Accurate Readings:
  • Always check the expiration date on test strips. Expired strips give unreliable results.
  • Store strips in their original container with the cap closed — exposure to moisture, heat, or light degrades them.
  • Calibrate your meter if it uses a code chip (many modern meters auto-code).
  • Periodically verify your meter's accuracy with a control solution provided by the manufacturer.
  • A meter reading within 15% of a laboratory value is considered acceptable (ISO 15197 standard).

Paired Testing

Paired testing means checking glucose before a meal and then 2 hours after the start of that meal. The difference reveals the glycemic impact of the meal. This is a powerful technique for learning which foods cause glucose spikes and for adjusting portions or medication.

📱 Continuous Glucose Monitoring (CGM)

What Is CGM?

A CGM system uses a tiny sensor inserted under the skin (usually on the arm or abdomen) that measures glucose in the interstitial fluid every 1–5 minutes, providing near-continuous glucose data. The sensor transmits readings to a receiver, smartphone app, or insulin pump.

Benefits of CGM

  • Trend arrows: Show the direction and rate of glucose change — rising fast, rising slowly, stable, falling slowly, falling fast — enabling proactive action.
  • Alarms and alerts: Warn of impending or current hypoglycemia and hyperglycemia, including overnight.
  • Comprehensive data: Captures 24-hour glucose profiles, revealing patterns invisible to fingerstick testing (overnight dips, dawn phenomenon, post-meal spikes).
  • Reduced fingersticks: Most CGMs are approved for treatment decisions without confirmatory fingerstick testing.
  • Improved outcomes: Studies show CGM use lowers HbA1c by 0.3–0.5%, reduces time in hypoglycemia, and improves time in range.
CGM Lag Time: CGM sensors measure glucose in interstitial fluid, not blood. There is a physiological lag of approximately 5–15 minutes between blood glucose changes and interstitial glucose changes. This means during rapidly changing glucose levels (e.g., after eating or during exercise), the CGM reading may not match a fingerstick reading exactly.

⏰ Time in Range (TIR)

Time in Range is an increasingly important metric made possible by CGM. It represents the percentage of time glucose is within a target range, typically 70–180 mg/dL (3.9–10.0 mmol/L).

A TIR of 70% corresponds approximately to an HbA1c of 7.0%. Each 5% increase in TIR is associated with clinically meaningful reductions in microvascular complications. Equally important, minimizing time below range reduces dangerous hypoglycemic episodes.

Ambulatory Glucose Profile (AGP): The AGP report is a standardized way to visualize CGM data. It displays the median glucose line, interquartile range (25th–75th percentile), and 5th–95th percentile bands over a 24-hour period, aggregating 14 days of data. This makes it easy to spot patterns like dawn phenomenon, post-meal spikes, or overnight lows.

📊 Recording and Recognizing Patterns

Keeping a Log

Whether using SMBG or CGM, maintaining a glucose log is essential. Record:

  • Date, time, and glucose reading
  • Context: fasting, pre-meal, post-meal (note which meal), bedtime, overnight
  • Carbohydrates consumed (grams or portions)
  • Insulin doses or oral medications taken
  • Physical activity (type, duration, intensity)
  • Illness, stress, menstrual cycle phase, or other relevant factors

Rule of Patterns: Do not adjust medication based on a single reading. Look for patterns across at least 3 days of data at the same time point before making changes. Avoid "stacking" corrections — wait at least 3–4 hours between rapid-acting insulin doses to prevent overlapping and hypoglycemia.

⚡ Special Situations

Monitoring During Illness (Sick Day Rules)

  • Check blood glucose every 2–4 hours.
  • Check blood or urine ketones if glucose > 250 mg/dL (especially for T1D).
  • Never stop insulin during illness — insulin needs often increase.
  • Stay hydrated with water or sugar-free fluids.
  • Contact your healthcare team if glucose > 300 mg/dL persistently, ketones are positive, you cannot keep fluids down, or you feel confused.

Monitoring During Exercise

  • Check glucose before, during (every 30–60 min for prolonged activity), and after exercise.
  • If glucose < 100 mg/dL before exercise, eat 15–30 g of carbs before starting.
  • If glucose > 250 mg/dL with ketones present, do not exercise until ketones clear.
  • Late-onset hypoglycemia can occur 6–12 hours after intense exercise — monitor accordingly.

Monitoring During Pregnancy

  • Much tighter targets: fasting < 95 mg/dL, 1-hour post-meal < 140 mg/dL, 2-hour post-meal < 120 mg/dL.
  • CGM use during pregnancy is supported by evidence (CONCEPTT trial showed reduced neonatal complications).
