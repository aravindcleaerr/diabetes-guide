💬 Disclosure Decisions

Whether to tell your employer or colleagues about your diabetes is a personal decision. Consider:

  • Reasons to disclose: safety (colleagues can help during hypoglycemia), ability to request accommodations, less stress from hiding self-care activities, legal protections may require disclosure
  • Reasons for caution: potential stigma, privacy concerns, worry about career impact
  • Who to tell: at minimum, inform your immediate supervisor and one trusted colleague who can recognize and assist with hypoglycemia
  • You are not legally obligated to disclose during interviews or hiring in most jurisdictions
  • If you do disclose, keep it factual and brief — "I have diabetes, it is well-managed, and I may occasionally need to check my blood sugar or eat a snack"
In India, the Rights of Persons with Disabilities Act (2016) and in many other countries, disability discrimination laws protect people with diabetes from workplace discrimination. Know your local laws.

📉 Managing Blood Sugar at Work

  • Keep your glucose meter (or rely on your CGM) easily accessible at your desk or workstation
  • Set phone alarms for testing and medication times
  • If blood sugar is high after lunch, a brief 10–15 minute walk can help bring it down
  • Stay hydrated — keep a water bottle at your desk
  • Learn how work-related stress affects your blood sugar and plan accordingly
  • If using insulin, identify a private space for injections if needed (though most people inject discreetly at their desk)

🍱 Snack and Meal Planning

  • Pack your lunch — this gives you full control over ingredients and portions
  • Keep a "diabetes drawer" at work with emergency supplies:
    • Glucose tablets or juice boxes (for hypoglycemia)
    • Healthy snacks: nuts, roasted chana, peanut butter, whole-grain crackers
    • A spare glucose meter and strips
  • If eating in the cafeteria, choose grilled over fried, extra vegetables, controlled portions of rice/bread
  • Avoid skipping meals due to meetings — eat on schedule or have a snack beforehand
  • For office celebrations (birthday cake, festival sweets), take a small portion or politely decline — have a prepared response ready
Meal-prepping on weekends saves time during the workweek and ensures you always have a diabetes-friendly lunch available.

🏋️ Desk Exercises

Prolonged sitting worsens insulin resistance. Incorporate movement every 30–60 minutes:

  • Seated leg raises — extend one leg, hold for 10 seconds, alternate; 10 reps each
  • Chair squats — stand up from your chair and sit back down slowly; 10 reps
  • Calf raises — stand behind your chair, rise on toes, hold, lower; 15 reps
  • Desk push-ups — lean against the desk edge and do push-ups; 10 reps
  • Walking meetings — suggest walking instead of sitting for one-on-one discussions
  • Stair climbing — take stairs instead of the elevator
  • Standing desk — alternate between sitting and standing if available
  • Set a timer to stand and stretch every 30 minutes

🌃 Shift Work Challenges

Shift work (especially rotating and night shifts) significantly disrupts diabetes management:

  • Circadian disruption — insulin sensitivity varies throughout the day; night shift workers often have higher insulin resistance
  • Meal timing — irregular eating patterns make blood sugar unpredictable
  • Sleep deprivation — worsens insulin resistance and increases appetite
  • Medication timing — when to take once-daily medications becomes complicated

Tips for Shift Workers

  • Work with your doctor to create a medication schedule tailored to your shift pattern
  • Eat regular, balanced meals even during night shifts — avoid relying on vending machines
  • Pack healthy meals and snacks for every shift
  • Monitor blood sugar more frequently during shift transitions
  • Prioritize sleep — use blackout curtains, earplugs, and maintain a consistent sleep routine
  • If on insulin, basal-bolus regimens are more flexible than fixed-dose regimens for shift workers
Rotating shifts are the most disruptive. If possible, request fixed shifts (even night shifts are easier to manage than rotating ones).

⚖️ Legal Rights and Accommodations

People with diabetes are entitled to reasonable workplace accommodations in most countries:

Accommodations You Can Request

  • Permission to check blood sugar and take medications at your workstation
  • Scheduled breaks for meals and snacks (consistent timing)
  • Access to a private space for insulin injections if preferred
  • A place to store insulin (refrigerator access)
  • Permission to keep food and glucose tablets at your desk
  • Flexible scheduling for medical appointments
  • Modified shift patterns if rotating shifts are causing dangerous blood sugar instability
  • Rest breaks if experiencing hypoglycemia
Document your needs with a letter from your doctor and submit a formal accommodation request through HR. Keep copies of all correspondence.

🚨 Dealing with Emergencies at Work

Hypoglycemia

  • Keep glucose tablets or juice at your desk and on your person at all times
  • Teach at least one colleague to recognize hypoglycemia symptoms (confusion, sweating, shaking, pale skin)
  • Show them where your glucose tablets and glucagon kit are stored
  • Wear a medical ID bracelet
  • If you feel low, treat immediately — do not try to finish a task first

Hyperglycemia / DKA

  • If blood sugar is persistently above 300 mg/dL with symptoms (nausea, rapid breathing, confusion), seek medical attention
  • Stay hydrated and take correction insulin if prescribed
Create a simple one-page "emergency card" for your desk or locker with: your diagnosis, medications, emergency contact, doctor's number, and instructions for colleagues on what to do if you are found unresponsive.

🧘 Stress Management at the Workplace

  • Identify your stress triggers — deadlines, conflicts, workload, commute
  • Use brief relaxation techniques: 2-minute deep breathing between tasks
  • Take your full lunch break — step away from your desk
  • Set realistic boundaries on workload and overtime
  • Use the Employee Assistance Program (EAP) if available for counseling
  • Maintain social connections with supportive colleagues
  • Separate work and personal life — avoid checking emails outside work hours
