Skin Care & Dermatological Conditions

Up to one-third of people with diabetes experience skin problems at some point. Many skin conditions can be the first sign of diabetes or an indicator of poor glycemic control.

Content last reviewed: January 2026 | AI-assisted content

🧬 Why Diabetes Affects the Skin

Diabetes impacts the skin through multiple mechanisms:

  • Impaired circulation: Reduced blood flow to the skin slows healing and nutrient delivery.
  • Neuropathy: Reduced sweating (anhidrosis) leads to dry, cracked skin; loss of sensation means injuries go unnoticed.
  • Immune dysfunction: Elevated glucose impairs white blood cell function, increasing infection susceptibility.
  • Dehydration: High blood glucose causes osmotic diuresis, dehydrating the skin.
  • Glycation: Advanced glycation end-products (AGEs) damage collagen and elastin, reducing skin elasticity.

Diabetic Dermopathy ("Shin Spots")

  • The most common skin manifestation of diabetes (up to 30% of patients).
  • Round or oval, light brown, scaly patches — usually on the shins.
  • Caused by changes to small blood vessels (microangiopathy).
  • Harmless and painless; no treatment needed. They may fade over time.
  • Often an indicator of other microvascular complications — worth mentioning to your doctor.

Acanthosis Nigricans

  • Dark, velvety, thickened patches of skin — most commonly in skin folds: neck, armpits, groin, knuckles.
  • Strongly associated with insulin resistance; often appears before diabetes is diagnosed.
  • Very common in Type 2 diabetes and obesity.
  • Not harmful itself, but a visible marker of metabolic dysfunction.
  • Improves with weight loss, exercise, and improved insulin sensitivity (e.g., metformin).

Bacterial Infections

  • Staphylococcal infections are most common: boils (furuncles), carbuncles, styes, and infected nail beds.
  • Cellulitis (spreading skin infection) is more frequent and can progress rapidly in diabetes.
  • Elevated blood glucose impairs neutrophil function (phagocytosis, chemotaxis).
  • Treatment: antibiotics (topical or systemic depending on severity) and glucose optimization.
  • Seek medical attention promptly for any skin infection — delays can lead to serious complications.

Fungal Infections

  • Candida: Thrives in warm, moist areas — skin folds (under breasts, groin, between toes), vaginal candidiasis, oral thrush, angular cheilitis.
  • Dermatophytes: Tinea pedis (athlete's foot), tinea cruris (jock itch), onychomycosis (nail fungus).
  • More common and more difficult to treat in diabetes.
  • SGLT2 inhibitors increase genital yeast infections due to glucosuria — maintain good hygiene.
  • Treatment: topical antifungals (clotrimazole, miconazole, terbinafine); oral antifungals for severe/nail infections.

Necrobiosis Lipoidica (Diabeticorum)

  • Rare but distinctive condition — shiny, red-brown or yellowish waxy plaques, usually on the shins.
  • May ulcerate centrally; skin becomes thin and atrophic, showing visible blood vessels.
  • More common in people assigned female at birth and in Type 1 diabetes.
  • Treatment is difficult: topical or intralesional corticosteroids, tacrolimus, phototherapy. Protect lesions from trauma.

Dry and Itchy Skin (Xerosis and Pruritus)

  • Very common — caused by autonomic neuropathy (reduced sweating), dehydration, and poor circulation.
  • Dry skin cracks and fissures become entry points for infection.
  • Generalized itching can also result from poor circulation or diabetic kidney disease.
  • Management: regular moisturizing, lukewarm (not hot) baths, gentle soap-free cleansers, humidifier use.

Slow Wound Healing

  • A hallmark feature of diabetes affecting all skin wounds.
  • Causes: impaired immune response, reduced blood flow, neuropathy, and impaired growth factor signaling.
  • Even minor cuts, scratches, and insect bites may take significantly longer to heal.
  • Prevention: keep glucose well controlled, protect skin from injury, treat wounds promptly.

Skin Tags (Acrochordons)

  • Small, soft, flesh-colored pedunculated growths — commonly on the neck, armpits, and eyelids.
  • Strongly correlated with insulin resistance and metabolic syndrome.
  • Harmless; can be removed for cosmetic reasons (cryotherapy, snip excision, electrocautery).

Other Conditions

  • Eruptive xanthomatosis: Firm, yellow, pea-sized bumps with red halos — associated with very high triglycerides. Resolves with lipid control.
  • Digital sclerosis: Thickened, waxy, tight skin on fingers and toes; reduced joint mobility. Related to glycation of collagen.
  • Bullosis diabeticorum: Painless blisters on hands, forearms, feet, or legs. Rare; heals spontaneously without scarring.
  • Granuloma annulare: Ring-shaped, raised reddish lesions — association with diabetes is debated.
  • Vitiligo: Autoimmune loss of skin pigmentation — more common in Type 1 diabetes.

🛡 Prevention and General Skin Care

  • Optimize glucose control: The single most important factor for preventing and managing diabetic skin conditions.
  • Moisturize daily: Apply fragrance-free lotion after bathing (avoid between toes).
  • Bathe wisely: Use lukewarm water and mild, soap-free cleansers. Avoid long, hot showers that strip natural oils.
  • Stay hydrated: Drink adequate water throughout the day.
  • Protect skin from injury: Wear gloves for housework/gardening, use sunscreen, avoid going barefoot.
  • Treat cuts and scrapes promptly: Clean with mild soap and water, apply antibiotic ointment, and cover with a sterile bandage.
  • Maintain good hygiene: Keep skin folds clean and dry (under breasts, groin, armpits).
  • Inspect your skin regularly: Look for new lesions, changes in existing ones, or signs of infection.
  • Avoid scratching: Itching can break the skin and introduce infection. Moisturize or use an anti-itch cream instead.

🩺 When to See a Dermatologist

  • Any wound that does not heal within 2 weeks despite proper care.
  • Signs of infection: increasing redness, warmth, swelling, pain, drainage, or red streaks.
  • New or changing skin lesions — rule out skin cancer (people with diabetes are not immune to melanoma).
  • Recurrent fungal or bacterial infections despite treatment.
  • Persistent itching without clear cause.
  • Suspected necrobiosis lipoidica or other uncommon diabetic skin conditions.
  • Acanthosis nigricans in a non-obese person (may warrant evaluation for other causes).
Take photographs of skin lesions over time to track changes. Share these with your dermatologist and diabetes care team for better-informed decisions.
