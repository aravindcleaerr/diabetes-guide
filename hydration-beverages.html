Alcohol has complex, potentially dangerous interactions with diabetes. If you drink, you must understand the risks:

Key Risks

  • Hypoglycemia (low blood sugar): The liver normally releases stored glucose to prevent low blood sugar. Alcohol blocks this process for up to 12â€“24 hours. This means severe hypoglycemia can occur hours after drinking â€” even the next morning.
  • Delayed hypoglycemia: You may go to bed with normal blood sugar after drinking, then develop dangerous hypoglycemia at 2â€“3 AM when the liver is still processing alcohol.
  • Masked symptoms: Alcohol intoxication symptoms (confusion, unsteadiness, slurred speech) mimic hypoglycemia. Others around you may think you are "just drunk" and not recognize a medical emergency.
  • Calorie load: Alcohol has 7 calories per gram (almost as much as fat). Beer and sweet wines contain significant carbohydrates that cause initial blood sugar spikes followed by delayed drops.
  • Medication interactions: Metformin + heavy alcohol increases lactic acidosis risk. Sulfonylureas + alcohol increases hypoglycemia risk. Alcohol can also cause facial flushing with some diabetes drugs.
Safe Alcohol Limits (If You Choose to Drink):
  • Lower limit (generally recommended for smaller body size): Maximum 1 standard drink per day
  • Upper limit (generally recommended for larger body size): Maximum 2 standard drinks per day
  • 1 standard drink = 350 mL beer (5% ABV) = 150 mL wine = 45 mL spirits (whiskey, vodka, rum)
  • NEVER drink on an empty stomach â€” always eat a carb-containing meal with alcohol
  • Check blood sugar before bed after drinking â€” eat a snack if below 120 mg/dL
  • Set an alarm to check blood sugar at 2â€“3 AM after an evening of drinking
  • Wear a medical ID â€” so others know you have diabetes if you become confused
  • Avoid sweet mixers: Use soda water, lime, or diet tonic water instead of regular tonic, cola, or fruit juice
When to NEVER Drink Alcohol:
  • If you have a history of severe hypoglycemia or hypoglycemia unawareness
  • If you have diabetic neuropathy (alcohol worsens nerve damage)
  • If you have liver disease or pancreatitis
  • If you are pregnant or trying to conceive
  • If you take metformin and have kidney impairment (lactic acidosis risk)
  • During fasting periods (no food to buffer the hypoglycemic effect)
