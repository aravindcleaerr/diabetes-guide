Infections & Immunity

Why diabetes increases infection risk and how to protect yourself

Content last reviewed: January 2026 | AI-assisted content

🧪 Why Diabetics Are More Prone to Infections

Diabetes impairs the immune system through multiple mechanisms, making infections more frequent, more severe, and harder to treat.

  • Impaired white blood cell function — Hyperglycemia reduces neutrophil chemotaxis, phagocytosis, and bacterial killing. WBCs become sluggish when blood glucose exceeds 200 mg/dL.
  • Poor circulation — Micro- and macro-vascular disease reduces blood flow, limiting delivery of immune cells and antibiotics to infection sites.
  • Neuropathy — Loss of sensation means injuries and early infections go unnoticed, especially on the feet.
  • High glucose in tissues — Elevated sugar in urine, skin, and mucous membranes provides a rich growth medium for bacteria and fungi.
  • Impaired complement activation — The complement cascade, a key arm of innate immunity, functions sub-optimally.
  • Biofilm formation — Glucose-rich environments promote bacterial biofilms on medical devices (catheters, implants).
Good glycemic control (HbA1c < 7%) significantly reduces infection risk and improves outcomes when infections do occur.

🦫 Common Infections in Diabetes

💧UTIs2-3x more common; glucosuria feeds bacteria in the urinary tract
🩹Skin & Soft TissueBoils, cellulitis, fungal infections; foot infections risk amputation
🦫Fungal InfectionsOral thrush, vaginal candidiasis, mucormycosis in severe cases
🫎RespiratoryHigher pneumonia, TB, influenza, and COVID-19 severity

Urinary Tract Infections (UTIs)

  • 2–3 times more common in people with diabetes
  • Glucosuria promotes bacterial growth in the urinary tract
  • Autonomic neuropathy causes incomplete bladder emptying (neurogenic bladder)
  • Higher risk of ascending infection (pyelonephritis) and complications like emphysematous pyelonephritis
  • SGLT2 inhibitors may slightly increase risk of genital and urinary infections

Skin and Soft Tissue Infections

  • Bacterial: boils (furuncles), carbuncles, cellulitis, abscesses
  • Fungal: candidiasis (groin, skin folds, under breasts), dermatophytosis (ringworm, athlete's foot)
  • Necrotizing fasciitis — a rare but life-threatening deep tissue infection; people with diabetes are disproportionately affected
  • Diabetic foot infections — the most feared complication, potentially leading to amputation

Fungal Infections

  • Oral thrush (candidiasis) — white patches in the mouth
  • Vaginal candidiasis — very common; recurrence suggests poor glycemic control
  • Mucormycosis (black fungus) — rare but aggressive; associated with uncontrolled diabetes and immunosuppression
  • Onychomycosis — fungal nail infections; harder to treat in people with diabetes

Respiratory Infections

  • Pneumonia — higher incidence and worse outcomes; Streptococcus pneumoniae and Klebsiella are common
  • Tuberculosis (TB) — risk is 2–3 times higher; diabetes worsens TB outcomes and TB worsens glucose control
  • Influenza — people with diabetes have higher hospitalization and mortality rates
  • COVID-19 — diabetes is a significant risk factor for severe disease

Other Infections

  • Malignant (necrotizing) otitis externa — severe ear infection, almost exclusively seen in older adults with diabetes
  • Emphysematous cholecystitis — gas-forming gallbladder infection
  • Periodontal (gum) disease — bidirectional relationship with glycemic control
  • Hepatitis B — people with diabetes are at risk due to shared glucose monitoring equipment

🛡 Prevention Strategies

  • Optimize blood glucose — the single most important preventive measure
  • Vaccinations — stay up to date (influenza yearly, pneumococcal, hepatitis B, COVID-19, shingles)
  • Hand hygiene — frequent handwashing with soap; use sanitizer when soap is unavailable
  • Skin care — keep skin clean and moisturized; treat cuts and abrasions promptly
  • Foot care — daily inspection, proper footwear, podiatrist visits
  • Dental hygiene — brush twice daily, floss, dental check-ups every 6 months
  • Avoid barefoot walking — even at home, wear protective footwear
  • Personal glucose monitors — never share lancets or monitoring devices
  • Promptly treat minor infections — do not wait for them to worsen

🦶 Foot Infection Warning Signs

Seek immediate medical attention if you notice any of the following on your feet:
  • Redness, warmth, or swelling around a wound or ulcer
  • Pus or foul-smelling discharge
  • Discoloration — blackened or darkened skin
  • Increasing pain or, conversely, sudden loss of pain (suggests deep tissue involvement)
  • Fever or chills alongside a foot wound
  • Red streaks spreading from the wound (lymphangitis)
  • Crepitus — a crackling sensation under the skin (suggests gas gangrene)
  • Non-healing wound for more than 2 weeks despite proper care

🚨 When to Seek Emergency Care

Go to the emergency room immediately if you have:
  • High fever (>101°F / 38.5°C) that does not respond to antipyretics
  • Rapidly spreading redness or swelling (mark the border with a pen to track progression)
  • Confusion, drowsiness, or altered consciousness
  • Blood sugar persistently >300 mg/dL with signs of illness
  • Symptoms of DKA: nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, fruity breath, rapid breathing
  • Signs of sepsis: rapid heartbeat, low blood pressure, cold/clammy skin, shivering

💊 Antibiotic Considerations

  • Infections in people with diabetes often require longer courses of antibiotics
  • Broader-spectrum antibiotics may be needed due to higher risk of polymicrobial or resistant organisms
  • Renal function must be checked before prescribing — many people with diabetes have reduced kidney function, requiring dose adjustments
  • Fluoroquinolones (e.g., ciprofloxacin) may cause unpredictable blood sugar fluctuations
  • Metformin may need to be temporarily held during severe infections, especially if there is risk of dehydration or renal impairment (lactic acidosis risk)
  • Some antibiotics (e.g., co-trimoxazole) can potentiate the effect of sulfonylureas, increasing hypoglycemia risk
  • Culture and sensitivity testing is preferred over empirical therapy whenever possible
Always inform your prescribing doctor about all diabetes medications you take, as several drug interactions exist between antibiotics and glucose-lowering drugs.
