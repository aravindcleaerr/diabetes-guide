🤰 Gestational Diabetes (GDM)

Gestational diabetes is glucose intolerance first recognized during pregnancy. It affects 5–15% of pregnancies and is becoming more common due to rising obesity rates and older maternal age.

Screening and Diagnosis

All pregnant individuals should be screened between 24–28 weeks of gestation. Those at high risk should be screened earlier (first trimester).

TestMethodDiagnostic Thresholds
75 g OGTT (WHO / IADPSG criteria)Fasting, 1-hour, and 2-hour glucose after 75 g glucose loadFasting ≥ 92 mg/dL, 1-hr ≥ 180 mg/dL, 2-hr ≥ 153 mg/dL (any one value meets criteria)
Two-step approach (ACOG)50 g glucose challenge test (GCT), followed by 100 g OGTT if GCT is abnormalGCT ≥ 130–140 mg/dL triggers OGTT; OGTT: two or more abnormal values (Carpenter-Coustan criteria)

Risk Factors

  • BMI > 25 (or > 23 in Asian populations)
  • Age > 25 years
  • Family history of type 2 diabetes (first-degree relative)
  • Previous GDM or macrosomic baby (>4 kg)
  • Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)
  • South Asian, Hispanic, African, or Indigenous ethnicity
  • History of unexplained stillbirth

📋 Management of GDM

Step 1: Medical Nutrition Therapy (MNT)

  • First-line treatment for all individuals with GDM
  • Calorie distribution: 33–40% carbohydrates, 20–25% protein, 35–40% healthy fats
  • Three small meals + 2–3 snacks distributed throughout the day
  • Emphasize low-glycemic-index carbohydrates (whole grains, legumes, vegetables)
  • Avoid refined sugars, fruit juices, and white bread/rice
  • Bedtime snack with protein and complex carbs to prevent overnight fasting ketosis

Step 2: Physical Activity

  • 30 minutes of moderate activity (brisk walking) most days, unless contraindicated
  • Post-meal walks (10–15 minutes) are particularly effective at reducing post-prandial spikes

Step 3: Insulin (if targets not met after 1–2 weeks of MNT)

  • Insulin is the preferred pharmacotherapy — it does not cross the placenta
  • Basal-bolus regimen or premixed insulin depending on the glucose pattern
  • Metformin may be used as an alternative (does cross the placenta; long-term offspring data still emerging)
  • Glyburide (glibenclamide) is falling out of favor due to higher neonatal hypoglycemia rates

📅 Preexisting Diabetes and Pregnancy Planning

If you have type 1 or type 2 diabetes and plan to become pregnant, prepare well in advance:

Pre-conception Goals

  • HbA1c < 6.5% (ideally < 6.0%) for at least 3 months before conception
  • Stable blood sugars with minimal hypoglycemia
  • Folic acid supplementation: 5 mg/day (higher dose than standard 0.4 mg) starting 3 months before conception
  • Comprehensive complication screening: eyes (retinopathy can worsen during pregnancy), kidneys, thyroid, blood pressure
Poorly controlled diabetes at conception increases the risk of major congenital malformations (heart defects, neural tube defects, skeletal abnormalities) by 3–5 fold. Optimization before conception is critical.

📈 Monitoring Schedule During Pregnancy

  • Blood sugar: self-monitor 4–7 times daily (fasting, pre-meal, 1-hour post-meal, bedtime). CGM is increasingly recommended.
  • HbA1c: check monthly during pregnancy (target < 6.0% if achievable without significant hypoglycemia)
  • Eye examination: each trimester if preexisting diabetes (retinopathy can progress rapidly)
  • Kidney function: each trimester
  • Blood pressure: every visit (pre-eclampsia risk is higher)
  • Fetal monitoring: regular ultrasounds for growth, anatomy scan at 18–20 weeks, growth scans in third trimester
  • Thyroid function: especially in type 1 diabetes (autoimmune thyroid disease is common)

🏥 Delivery Planning

  • Well-controlled GDM with no complications: delivery can be planned at 39–40 weeks
  • Insulin-requiring GDM or preexisting diabetes: delivery often planned at 37–39 weeks depending on control and complications
  • Macrosomia (estimated fetal weight > 4.5 kg): discuss elective cesarean to reduce birth injury risk
  • During labor: intravenous insulin and glucose infusion to maintain blood sugar 70–120 mg/dL
  • Insulin requirements drop dramatically immediately after delivery (placental hormones are gone) — doses are typically halved or returned to pre-pregnancy levels

🔍 Postpartum Screening for Type 2 Diabetes

Those who had GDM have a 50% lifetime risk of developing type 2 diabetes:

  • 75 g OGTT at 6–12 weeks postpartum — to check if glucose has normalized
  • If normal: repeat screening every 1–3 years for life
  • Lifestyle measures (healthy weight, regular exercise, balanced diet) reduce progression by up to 58%
  • Metformin can be considered for prevention in high-risk individuals
Do not assume that normal blood sugar after delivery means GDM is gone permanently. Lifelong surveillance is essential.

👶 Breastfeeding Benefits

  • Breastfeeding is strongly encouraged for those with diabetes (all types) who are nursing
  • Improves insulin sensitivity in the nursing parent
  • Helps with postpartum weight loss
  • Reduces the nursing parent's long-term risk of type 2 diabetes by up to 50%
  • Reduces the baby's future risk of obesity and type 2 diabetes
  • Insulin is safe during breastfeeding (it is not absorbed orally by the infant)
  • Metformin is considered safe during breastfeeding (minimal transfer to breast milk)
  • Monitor for hypoglycemia during and after breastfeeding (calorie expenditure can lower blood sugar)
Keep a snack and glass of water nearby during breastfeeding sessions. The caloric demand of lactation (~500 kcal/day) can cause unexpected blood sugar drops.
