Nerve Damage & Diabetic Neuropathy

Diabetic neuropathy affects up to 50% of people with diabetes. It manifests in many forms, from painful tingling in the feet to digestive and cardiovascular dysfunction.

⚡ Overview

Diabetic neuropathy is nerve damage caused by prolonged exposure to high blood glucose, along with metabolic factors such as dyslipidemia, oxidative stress, and inflammation. It can affect any nerve in the body but most commonly targets the longest nerves first — those running to the feet and legs (length-dependent pattern).

Risk increases with duration of diabetes, poor glycemic control, smoking, hypertension, and dyslipidemia. Up to 50% of neuropathy may be asymptomatic, discovered only on examination.

1. Distal Symmetric Polyneuropathy (Peripheral Neuropathy)

The most common form, affecting up to 50% of people with diabetes. It follows a "stocking-glove" distribution, starting in the toes and feet and gradually progressing upward.

  • Sensory symptoms: Numbness, tingling, burning, "pins and needles," shooting or stabbing pain, electric shock sensations, sensitivity to touch (allodynia).
  • Motor symptoms (later): Weakness in foot muscles, difficulty walking, foot deformities (hammer toes, claw toes).
  • Loss of protective sensation: The most dangerous consequence — inability to feel injuries, blisters, or pressure sores, leading to foot ulcers and amputations.
Paradoxically, the most dangerous feet are those that feel nothing. Loss of sensation (not pain) is the primary driver of diabetic foot ulcers and amputations. Daily foot inspection is essential.

3. Focal Neuropathies (Mononeuropathies)

  • Sudden onset; usually affect a single nerve.
  • Cranial neuropathies: Third nerve palsy (double vision, drooping eyelid with pupil sparing), sixth or seventh nerve palsy.
  • Entrapment neuropathies: Carpal tunnel syndrome (median nerve) — very common in diabetes; ulnar neuropathy; peroneal neuropathy (foot drop).
  • Most focal neuropathies resolve spontaneously over weeks to months.

4. Proximal Neuropathy (Diabetic Amyotrophy)

  • Also called diabetic lumbosacral radiculoplexus neuropathy.
  • Severe pain in the thigh, hip, or buttock, followed by weakness and wasting of thigh muscles.
  • Usually unilateral initially, may become bilateral.
  • Most common in older adults with Type 2 diabetes.
  • Often self-limiting over months to years, but recovery may be incomplete.
  • Treatment: pain management, physical therapy, glycemic control. Immunotherapy (IV immunoglobulin, corticosteroids) is sometimes used for severe cases.

🔍 Diagnosis

  • Clinical examination: Annual comprehensive foot exam including 10-g monofilament testing, vibration perception (128 Hz tuning fork), ankle reflexes, and pinprick sensation.
  • Nerve conduction studies (NCS) and electromyography (EMG): Confirm diagnosis and severity; distinguish from other causes of neuropathy.
  • Autonomic function tests: Heart rate variability, Valsalva maneuver response, tilt-table testing for orthostatic hypotension.
  • Exclude other causes: B12 deficiency (especially if on metformin), hypothyroidism, alcohol, chronic kidney disease, CIDP, vasculitis.

Alpha-Lipoic Acid

  • A potent antioxidant that has shown benefit in some clinical trials (SYDNEY, NATHAN studies), particularly at 600 mg/day IV or orally.
  • May improve neuropathic symptoms and nerve conduction.
  • Well-tolerated with few side effects (GI upset at high doses).
  • Approved for diabetic neuropathy treatment in several European countries.
  • Evidence is moderate — consider as an adjunct, not a replacement for standard therapy.

🧙 Non-Pharmacological Approaches

  • Physical therapy: Balance training, strengthening exercises, gait training to reduce fall risk.
  • TENS (transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation): May provide symptomatic relief for some patients.
  • Acupuncture: Limited evidence but some patients report benefit.
  • Cognitive behavioral therapy: Helps with the psychological burden of chronic pain.
  • Regular exercise: Improves nerve function, blood flow, and pain perception. Even walking helps.

🦶 The Importance of Foot Care

Peripheral neuropathy is the primary risk factor for diabetic foot ulcers, which precede 85% of diabetes-related amputations. Because sensation is diminished or lost, injuries go unnoticed and infections progress rapidly.

  • Inspect feet daily (use a mirror for the soles).
  • Never walk barefoot.
  • Wear properly fitted shoes and diabetic socks.
  • See a podiatrist regularly.
  • Report any wound, blister, or color change immediately.
