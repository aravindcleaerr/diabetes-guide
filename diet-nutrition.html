📊 Glycemic Index & Glycemic Load

The Glycemic Index (GI) ranks carbohydrate-containing foods on a scale of 0 to 100 based on how quickly they raise blood glucose after eating. Pure glucose is the reference at GI = 100.

CategoryGI RangeExamples
Low GI0 – 55Lentils (29), chickpeas (33), apples (36), barley (28), milk (31)
Medium GI56 – 69Basmati rice (58), whole-wheat bread (62), sweet potato (63), banana (62)
High GI70 – 100White bread (75), white rice (73), cornflakes (81), watermelon (76)

Glycemic Load (GL) gives a more complete picture by factoring in the amount of carbohydrate in a typical serving:

GL = (GI × grams of carbohydrate per serving) ÷ 100

  • Low GL: 0 – 10
  • Medium GL: 11 – 19
  • High GL: 20+
A food can have a high GI but a low GL if a typical serving contains little carbohydrate. Watermelon has a GI of 76, but its GL per serving is only about 5 because a slice contains very few carbs.

🍽️ The Diabetes Plate Method

Recommended by the American Diabetes Association, the plate method is the simplest way to build balanced meals without weighing or counting:

  1. Use a 9-inch (23 cm) dinner plate.
  2. Fill half the plate with non-starchy vegetables.
  3. Fill one quarter with lean protein.
  4. Fill one quarter with carbohydrate foods (grains, starchy vegetables, or fruit).
  5. Choose water or a zero-calorie drink on the side.
This method automatically controls portions and balances macronutrients without complex calculations.

✅ Foods to Eat (Encouraged)

Non-Starchy Vegetables

These are very low in calories and carbohydrates and rich in fibre, vitamins, and minerals. Aim for variety and colour.

  • Leafy greens — spinach, kale, lettuce, methi (fenugreek leaves), amaranth
  • Cruciferous — broccoli, cauliflower, cabbage, Brussels sprouts
  • Others — bell peppers, tomatoes, cucumber, zucchini, mushrooms, okra (bhindi), bitter gourd (karela), bottle gourd (lauki)

Lean Protein

  • Skinless poultry (chicken breast, turkey)
  • Fish and seafood (especially fatty fish like salmon, mackerel, sardines for omega-3)
  • Eggs
  • Legumes and lentils (dal, rajma, chana, moong)
  • Tofu, paneer (in moderation), tempeh
  • Low-fat or fat-free dairy (curd/yoghurt, buttermilk)

Healthy Fats

  • Nuts — almonds, walnuts, pistachios (handful/day)
  • Seeds — flaxseeds, chia seeds, pumpkin seeds, sunflower seeds
  • Avocado
  • Olive oil, mustard oil (in moderation)
  • Fatty fish (salmon, mackerel)

Whole Grains

  • Oats (steel-cut or rolled, not instant flavoured)
  • Brown rice, quinoa, barley
  • Millets — ragi, jowar, bajra
  • Whole-wheat products (in controlled portions)

🚫 Foods to Limit or Avoid

Refined Carbohydrates

  • White rice — high GI (73); swap for brown rice, cauliflower rice, or millets
  • White bread, maida-based products — naan, biscuits, cakes, pastries
  • Refined cereals — cornflakes, instant oats with added sugar

Sugary Drinks

  • Regular soda and cola (a 350 ml can has ~39 g sugar)
  • Packaged fruit juices (often contain as much sugar as soda)
  • Sweet tea, flavoured coffee drinks, energy drinks
  • Sweetened lassi, sharbat, rooh afza

Fruit Juices (Even 100% Juice)

Juicing removes fibre and concentrates sugar. A glass of orange juice has ~26 g of sugar and negligible fibre, while a whole orange has ~12 g of sugar plus 3 g of fibre that slows absorption.

Even “no added sugar” fruit juices can spike blood glucose rapidly. Prefer eating whole fruits in moderation (1–2 servings/day).

Other Foods to Limit

  • Fried foods (samosa, pakora, chips)
  • Processed meats (sausages, bacon, salami)
  • Sweets, mithai, chocolates, ice cream
  • Honey, jaggery (gur) — these still raise blood sugar despite being “natural”
  • Excessive salt (<5 g/day recommended by WHO)

📏 Portion Control

Even healthy foods can raise blood sugar if eaten in excess. Practical portion strategies include:

  • Use smaller plates and bowls — a 9-inch plate instead of a 12-inch plate naturally reduces intake by 20–25%.
  • Hand method: palm = protein portion, fist = carb portion, cupped hand = fruit/grains, thumb tip = fats/oils.
  • Measure once, memorise always: use measuring cups for a week to calibrate your visual estimates.
  • Don’t eat from the packet — serve a fixed portion on a plate.
  • Eat slowly — it takes ~20 minutes for satiety signals to reach the brain.

🌾 The Importance of Fibre

Dietary fibre slows carbohydrate digestion, reduces post-meal glucose spikes, improves cholesterol, and promotes satiety. The recommended intake is 25–30 g per day for adults.

Ghee and coconut oil are often promoted as “superfoods” but are high in saturated fat. Use them sparingly — no more than 1–2 teaspoons per day.

🔎 Reading Food Labels

Learning to read nutrition labels is essential for making informed choices. Focus on these key items:

Step-by-Step Label Reading

  1. Check serving size — All values on the label are per serving. If you eat double the serving, double the numbers.
  2. Total carbohydrates — This is the most important number for blood sugar management. It includes sugars, fibre, and starches.
  3. Added sugars — Look for this separately. WHO recommends <25 g of added sugar per day.
  4. Dietary fibre — Higher is better. Choose products with ≥3 g fibre per serving.
  5. Total fat and type — Check for trans fat (should be 0 g). Prefer products low in saturated fat.
  6. Sodium — Keep below 2,300 mg/day (ideally <1,500 mg if you have hypertension).
  7. Ingredient list — Ingredients are listed in descending order of weight. If sugar (or its aliases) is in the first 3 ingredients, avoid the product.

Hidden Names for Sugar

Sugar goes by many names on labels: sucrose, high-fructose corn syrup, dextrose, maltose, cane sugar, agave nectar, rice syrup, malt syrup, molasses, honey, fruit juice concentrate, and many more. If the label lists multiple types of sugar, the total sugar content may be very high.

A quick rule: if the product has more than 5 g of added sugar per serving, look for a better alternative.
