🔢 Carbohydrate Counting Basics

Carbohydrate counting (carb counting) is a meal-planning method where you track the grams of carbohydrate you eat at each meal and snack. Carbohydrates have the greatest impact on blood sugar.

  • 1 carb serving = 15 g of carbohydrate
  • Most adults with diabetes aim for 3–4 carb servings (45–60 g) per meal and 1–2 servings (15–30 g) per snack, but this varies by individual needs.
  • People on insulin use carb counting to calculate mealtime doses (insulin-to-carb ratio).

🍽️ The Plate Method (Detailed)

The simplest approach to balanced eating, requiring no scales or apps. Use a standard 9-inch (23 cm) plate.

  • ½ plate — Non-starchy vegetables: Salad, spinach, broccoli, bhindi, lauki, tinda, karela, capsicum, cabbage, tomato, cucumber.
  • ¼ plate — Lean protein: Grilled chicken/fish, dal, paneer, tofu, eggs, curd.
  • ¼ plate — Carbohydrates: Brown rice, roti, millet roti, sweet potato, corn, fruit.
  • Drink: Water, unsweetened chaas, green tea, black coffee.
The plate method automatically limits carbs to about 45 g per meal without counting — ideal for those who find carb counting tedious.

⏰ Meal Timing

When you eat is nearly as important as what you eat for blood sugar stability.

  • Don’t skip meals — skipping leads to low blood sugar followed by overeating and high blood sugar. This roller-coaster worsens overall control.
  • Eat every 4–5 hours to maintain steady glucose levels.
  • Aim for consistent timing: Try to eat meals at roughly the same times each day. This helps with medication dosing and insulin action.
  • Don’t eat too late: Finish dinner at least 2–3 hours before bedtime. Late-night eating is associated with higher fasting glucose.
  • Breakfast is important: Studies show that skipping breakfast increases post-lunch glucose spikes by 20–30%.

🍴 Eating Out Tips

  • Check the menu online before going — plan what to order in advance.
  • Start with a salad or soup to reduce appetite before the main course.
  • Ask for sauces and dressings on the side — these often contain hidden sugar.
  • Choose grilled, baked, steamed, or tandoori over fried items.
  • Avoid buffets if possible — they encourage overeating. If at a buffet, use a small plate and fill it once.
  • Skip the bread basket / papad at the start of the meal.
  • Request half portions or share an entrée.
  • Avoid sugary drinks — order water, lime soda (unsweetened), or chaas.
  • At restaurants: Tandoori chicken/fish, dal, paneer tikka, and salads are usually safe choices. Avoid butter chicken, naan, biryani in large portions, and creamy gravies.
  • Box half your meal to take home if portions are large.
It is perfectly fine to eat out occasionally. The goal is not perfection but making the best available choices consistently.
