Travel Tips

How to travel safely and confidently with diabetes

Content last reviewed: January 2026 | AI-assisted content

🎒 Quick Packing Checklist

  • Doctor's letter & prescription copies
  • Medical alert ID bracelet
  • Insulin (2x supply) in hand luggage
  • Insulin cooling case (Frio wallet)
  • Glucose meter + extra strips + batteries
  • CGM / pump supplies
  • Syringes / pen needles (2x supply)
  • Glucose tablets / gel + glucagon kit
  • Ketone strips
  • Snacks for delays and hypos
  • Travel insurance documents
  • Time-zone insulin adjustment plan

📝 Planning Ahead

  • Medical letter — get a letter from your doctor on letterhead stating your diagnosis, medications, and the need to carry supplies (syringes, insulin, lancets). Carry it in English and the local language if traveling internationally.
  • Prescriptions — carry copies of all prescriptions with generic drug names (brand names vary by country)
  • Medical ID — wear a medical alert bracelet or necklace at all times
  • Pre-travel check-up — see your doctor 4–6 weeks before travel to review control, update vaccinations, and discuss destination-specific risks
  • Research your destination — know where pharmacies and hospitals are located; save addresses and phone numbers
  • Travel insurance — essential; ensure diabetes and its complications are covered; read the fine print regarding pre-existing conditions

🧳 Carrying Supplies

Pack at least twice the amount of supplies you expect to need. Delays, lost luggage, and extended trips happen.
  • Insulin (vials or pens) — carry in hand luggage, never in checked baggage (cargo hold temperatures can freeze or overheat insulin)
  • Glucose meter, test strips, lancets, and extra batteries
  • CGM sensors and transmitter if applicable
  • Insulin pump supplies (infusion sets, reservoirs, backup pump batteries)
  • Syringes or pen needles
  • Glucose tablets or gel for hypoglycemia treatment
  • Glucagon emergency kit
  • Ketone testing strips
  • Sharps disposal container (small travel-size)
Split supplies between two bags — if one is lost or stolen, you still have a backup.

❄️ Insulin Storage During Travel

  • Insulin in use can be kept at room temperature (below 30°C / 86°F) for 28 days (check specific product labeling)
  • Spare insulin must be refrigerated (2–8°C / 36–46°F)
  • Cooling cases: use insulated travel cases (e.g., Frio cooling wallets) that use evaporative cooling — no electricity needed
  • Never leave insulin in a car (dashboard, glove box) — temperatures can exceed 50°C
  • Do not place insulin directly against ice packs — freezing destroys insulin
  • In hot climates, request a hotel room with a mini-fridge
  • Inspect insulin before use — if cloudy (clear insulin), discolored, or contains particles, discard it

🕰️ Time Zone Adjustments for Insulin

Crossing time zones requires careful insulin timing adjustments:

Traveling East (shorter day)

  • The day is shorter — you may need less basal insulin
  • If using once-daily basal insulin, reduce the dose proportionally for the shortened day
  • Resume normal dose and timing at your destination

Traveling West (longer day)

  • The day is longer — you may need more basal insulin or supplemental rapid-acting doses
  • If using once-daily basal, you may need a small supplemental dose
  • Resume normal dose and timing at your destination

General Rules

  • For time changes of less than 3 hours: simply shift your schedule gradually (1 hour/day)
  • For time changes of more than 3 hours: discuss a specific plan with your doctor before travel
  • Pump users: change the pump clock to local time upon arrival
  • Monitor blood sugar more frequently during travel and for 2–3 days after arriving
Write down your insulin schedule for each leg of the journey, including dose adjustments, to avoid confusion during travel fatigue.

🛡️ Airport Security and Diabetes Supplies

  • Diabetes supplies, including syringes and insulin, are permitted through airport security in most countries
  • Inform the security officer that you have diabetes supplies before screening
  • Keep supplies in a clear, labeled bag for easy inspection
  • Carry your doctor's letter and prescriptions
  • Insulin pumps and CGMs: request a hand-pat inspection instead of going through full-body scanners if preferred (some manufacturers advise against X-ray or scanner exposure)
  • Never put insulin in checked luggage — it may freeze in the cargo hold

🍫 Snack Packing

Always carry snacks in case of delays, missed meals, or hypoglycemia:

  • For hypoglycemia: glucose tablets (most reliable), hard candies, small juice boxes
  • For meal delays: nuts, peanut butter crackers, protein bars, cheese and crackers, whole-grain biscuits
  • Pack more than you think you need — travel is unpredictable
  • Check customs regulations for food items if traveling internationally

🚶 Staying Active During Travel

  • Walk around the airport during layovers
  • On long flights, get up and walk the aisle every 1–2 hours (also reduces DVT risk)
  • Do seated exercises: ankle circles, calf raises, knee lifts
  • At your destination, explore on foot — walking tours combine sightseeing with exercise
  • Use hotel gyms, pools, or simply walk in the area
  • Maintain your exercise routine as closely as possible
Wear comfortable, well-fitting shoes when traveling — blisters and foot injuries are especially dangerous for people with diabetes and neuropathy. Bring a spare pair.

🏥 Destination Medical Facilities

  • Research hospital locations and emergency numbers before departure
  • Learn how to say "I have diabetes" and "I need sugar/insulin" in the local language
  • Save the address of the nearest pharmacy and hospital to your phone
  • Know the local equivalent of your medications (use generic names)
  • Consider the International Association for Medical Assistance to Travellers (IAMAT) for a network of English-speaking doctors worldwide
