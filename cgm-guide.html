Continuous Glucose Monitors (CGM)

Real-time glucose tracking — how CGMs work, popular devices, and getting the most from your data

Content last reviewed: January 2026 | AI-assisted content

📡 What is a CGM?

A Continuous Glucose Monitor (CGM) is a device that tracks glucose levels throughout the day and night, providing near-continuous data without the need for frequent fingersticks. Unlike traditional blood glucose meters that give you a single snapshot, CGMs reveal the complete picture of your glucose patterns.

Interstitial vs. Blood Glucose: CGMs measure glucose in the interstitial fluid (the fluid between cells), not directly in the blood. This is physiologically different from capillary blood glucose measured by fingerstick meters. Most of the time, the values are very close, but they can diverge during rapidly changing glucose levels.

⚙️ How CGMs Work

A CGM system consists of three main components working together to deliver continuous glucose data:

1. The Sensor

  • A tiny, flexible filament (about the width of a hair) inserted just under the skin.
  • Contains an enzyme (glucose oxidase) that reacts with glucose in the interstitial fluid.
  • Generates an electrical signal proportional to the glucose concentration.
  • Lasts 7–14 days (depending on the system) before replacement is needed.
  • Insertion is quick (usually painless) using an automatic applicator.

2. The Transmitter

  • A small device that attaches to the sensor (some systems have integrated transmitter + sensor).
  • Converts the electrical signal from the sensor into glucose readings.
  • Wirelessly sends data to the receiver/smartphone via Bluetooth.
  • Powered by a battery — reusable transmitters last 3–12 months; some are disposable with the sensor.

3. The Receiver/Display

  • Can be a dedicated handheld receiver device, a smartphone app, or a smartwatch.
  • Displays current glucose, trend arrows, and historical graphs.
  • Triggers audio/vibration alerts for highs, lows, and rapid changes.
  • Stores data for review and sharing with healthcare providers.

Factory Calibration vs. User Calibration

Modern CGMs come in two calibration types:

  • Factory-calibrated (no fingerstick required): FreeStyle Libre, Dexcom G6/G7, Medtronic Guardian 4. These are calibrated during manufacturing and require no user calibration.
  • User-calibrated: Older systems (Dexcom G5, Medtronic Guardian 3) and Eversense E3 require 1–2 fingerstick calibrations per day to maintain accuracy.
How accurate are CGMs? Accuracy is measured by Mean Absolute Relative Difference (MARD). Modern factory-calibrated CGMs have MARD values of 8–10%, meaning readings are typically within 8–10% of a lab-quality blood glucose measurement. This is accurate enough for treatment decisions in most situations.

🎯 Understanding CGM Data

Glucose Variability

Variability refers to how much your glucose swings up and down. Even if your average glucose is good, high variability (frequent spikes and drops) is associated with increased risk of complications and hypoglycemia. The Coefficient of Variation (CV) should ideally be ≤ 36%.

Ambulatory Glucose Profile (AGP)

The AGP is a standardized report that combines 14 days of CGM data into a single 24-hour graph:

  • Median line (50th percentile): Your "typical" glucose at each time of day.
  • Interquartile range (25th–75th percentile): The middle 50% of readings — where you spend most of your time.
  • 10th–90th percentile bands: The wider range capturing most of your variability.

AGP makes patterns immediately visible: dawn phenomenon appears as a morning rise, post-meal spikes show as peaks after mealtimes, and overnight lows appear as dips during sleep hours.

TIR and HbA1c: A Time in Range of 70% roughly corresponds to an HbA1c of 7.0%. Each 5% increase in TIR is associated with clinically meaningful reductions in diabetes complications. TIR gives you actionable, real-time feedback that HbA1c (measured every 3 months) cannot provide.

⏰ CGM vs. Fingerstick — When You Still Need to Prick

While CGMs reduce the need for fingersticks dramatically, there are situations where a confirmatory fingerstick is still valuable:

When to Confirm with Fingerstick

  • Symptoms don't match the CGM reading: If you feel hypoglycemic but CGM shows normal glucose (or vice versa), always trust your symptoms and confirm with a fingerstick.
  • Before treating hypoglycemia (if unsure): If CGM shows low but you feel fine, confirm before eating glucose.
  • Rapidly changing glucose: During the 5–15 minute lag time (see below), CGM may not reflect actual blood glucose during fast rises or falls.
  • Sensor warmup period: The first 1–12 hours after insertion may be less accurate.
  • Compression artifacts: If you've been lying on the sensor, readings may be falsely low.
  • Critical decisions: Before driving when glucose is borderline low, or before giving a large insulin correction.

Understanding Lag Time

CGM readings lag behind actual blood glucose by approximately 5–15 minutes. This is because:

  • Glucose moves from blood into interstitial fluid, then is detected by the sensor.
  • The CGM algorithm applies smoothing and filtering, adding slight delay.

During stable glucose, this lag is clinically insignificant. During rapid changes (after eating, during exercise, after insulin injection), the lag matters more. This is why trend arrows are so valuable — they tell you where glucose is heading, not just where it is now.

Safety first: When in doubt, do a fingerstick. CGMs are highly accurate for most situations, but no technology is perfect. If something doesn't feel right, trust your body and verify.

🔔 Alerts and Alarms

CGM alerts can be lifesaving, but they can also become overwhelming if not set appropriately. Finding the right balance prevents "alarm fatigue."

Types of Alerts

  • Low glucose alert: Triggers when glucose falls below a set threshold (commonly 70–80 mg/dL).
  • Urgent low alert: Triggers at a critically low level (typically 55 mg/dL) — this one should never be silenced.
  • High glucose alert: Triggers when glucose rises above a set threshold (commonly 180–250 mg/dL).
  • Predictive low alert: Warns you that glucose is projected to go low in the next 10–20 minutes, giving you time to act.
  • Rate of change alerts: Warns when glucose is rising or falling rapidly.
  • Signal loss alert: Notifies when the receiver has lost communication with the transmitter.

Avoiding Alarm Fatigue

  • Start with wider thresholds: If you get too many alerts, you'll start ignoring them. Begin with generous settings and tighten gradually.
  • Use snooze appropriately: Snooze an alert if you've already taken action and need time for it to work — but don't snooze indefinitely.
  • Customize for time of day: Some CGMs allow different thresholds for day vs. night. You might want stricter overnight low alerts.
  • Prioritize low alerts: High alerts can be annoying but are rarely dangerous in the short term. Low alerts are critical for safety.
  • Review alert history: If you're getting dozens of alerts daily, work with your diabetes team to address the underlying patterns rather than just silencing alarms.
Never disable the urgent low alert. This alert can be lifesaving, especially overnight. Alarm fatigue is a real problem, but the solution is to improve glucose control and adjust other settings — not to silence critical safety alarms.

🩹 Sensor Placement and Care

Approved Sensor Sites

  • FreeStyle Libre: Back of upper arm only.
  • Dexcom G6/G7: Abdomen (adults), upper buttocks (children 2–17), back of upper arm (with prescription for off-label use in some countries).
  • Medtronic Guardian: Abdomen, back of upper arm.
  • Eversense: Upper arm (implanted by healthcare provider).

Tips for Successful Sensor Application

  1. Clean the site: Use soap and water or an alcohol wipe. Let the skin dry completely before application — moisture affects adhesion.
  2. Avoid problem areas: Don't place sensors on scars, moles, stretch marks, tattoos, or areas with lipohypertrophy (from insulin injections).
  3. Rotate sites: Don't apply a new sensor in exactly the same spot as the previous one. Rotate within the approved area to prevent skin irritation.
  4. Press firmly: After insertion, press the adhesive patch firmly for 10–30 seconds to ensure good contact.
  5. Consider skin prep: If adhesion is a problem, use a skin barrier wipe (e.g., Skin-Tac, IV Prep) before application — let it dry completely first.

Keeping the Sensor Secure

  • Overpatch/cover patch: An additional adhesive patch placed over the sensor. Most CGM manufacturers provide these free of charge.
  • Medical tape: Hypafix, Tegaderm, or KT tape can reinforce edges that start to lift.
  • Armband/body tape: For arm sensors, a fabric band can provide extra security during sports.
  • Avoid direct water jets: While CGMs are water-resistant, direct shower jets or prolonged soaking can loosen adhesive.

Compression Lows

"Compression lows" occur when pressure on the sensor (lying on it, tight clothing, seatbelt) causes falsely low readings. The pressure reduces blood flow to the sensor area, lowering interstitial glucose temporarily.

  • These are usually obvious because glucose "drops" suddenly and recovers when you change position.
  • If you get unexplained low readings at night, check if you're sleeping on your sensor.
  • Confirm with a fingerstick before treating — compression lows are not real hypoglycemia.
Skin sensitivity: Some people develop skin irritation or allergic reactions to CGM adhesive. Solutions include: applying a barrier wipe (Cavilon, Flonase nasal spray) before the sensor, using a different brand of CGM, or applying a hydrocolloid patch as a buffer between skin and adhesive.

💡 Getting the Most from Your CGM

Pattern Recognition — Using Data to Improve Control

  • Review your data regularly: Spend 5–10 minutes weekly looking at your AGP and daily graphs. Look for patterns, not individual readings.
  • Use the 3-day rule: Only adjust insulin based on patterns that repeat for 3 or more days at the same time.
  • Focus on one thing at a time: If you have high fasting glucose AND post-lunch spikes, fix one first. Making multiple changes simultaneously makes it impossible to know what worked.
  • Correlate with events: Note what you ate, when you exercised, and how you felt. Many apps let you tag meals and activities.

Sharing Data with Your Doctor

  • Generate reports before appointments: All CGM systems can generate PDF reports (AGP, time in range, statistics). Email or print these for your doctor.
  • Share access: Many CGMs allow you to share real-time data with healthcare providers or family members via follower apps (LibreLinkUp, Dexcom Follow, CarePartner).
  • Cloud platforms: LibreView, Dexcom Clarity, and Glooko aggregate your data for easy review.
  • Come with questions: Circle specific patterns in your data and ask your doctor about them. This makes appointments more productive.

Integration with Insulin Pumps — Automated Insulin Delivery (AID)

The ultimate use of CGM data is integration with an insulin pump in a "closed-loop" or "hybrid closed-loop" system:

  • How it works: The CGM sends glucose data to the pump every 5 minutes. The pump algorithm automatically adjusts basal insulin delivery — increasing when glucose is rising, decreasing or suspending when glucose is falling.
  • Benefits: Significantly improved time in range (often > 70–80%), reduced hypoglycemia, less mental burden of constant decision-making.
  • Current systems in India: Medtronic 780G + Guardian 4, Tandem Control-IQ + Dexcom G6/G7, Omnipod 5 + Dexcom G6/G7 (availability varies).
  • Still requires user input: You must still enter carbohydrates and confirm boluses for meals. No system is fully autonomous yet.

DIY/Open-Source Loops

Some tech-savvy users build their own closed-loop systems using open-source software (Loop, OpenAPS, AndroidAPS) with compatible pumps and CGMs. While these can be highly effective, they require technical knowledge, are not FDA/regulatory approved, and should only be pursued with full understanding of the risks and responsibilities.

The future of CGM: Technology is advancing rapidly. Non-invasive CGMs (measuring glucose without a needle), longer-lasting implantable sensors, and fully closed-loop "artificial pancreas" systems are in development. Stay connected with your diabetes care team to learn about new options as they become available.
