Dental & Oral Health

Diabetes and oral health share a bidirectional relationship — each condition worsens the other. Proactive dental care is an often-overlooked but important part of diabetes management.

Content last reviewed: January 2026 | AI-assisted content

🔗 The Bidirectional Relationship

Diabetes impairs immune function and increases inflammation, making the gums more susceptible to infection. In turn, periodontal (gum) disease creates a chronic inflammatory state that worsens insulin resistance and makes blood glucose harder to control.

  • People with diabetes are 2-3 times more likely to develop periodontitis.
  • Severe periodontitis is associated with an increase in HbA1c of approximately 0.3-0.4%.
  • Treating periodontal disease has been shown to modestly improve HbA1c (by ~0.3-0.4%) in several studies.
  • Poor glycemic control accelerates gum disease progression, creating a vicious cycle.
Think of periodontal treatment as part of your diabetes management plan. Healthy gums contribute to better blood glucose control, and good glucose control protects your gums.

🩸 Periodontitis (Gum Disease)

Periodontitis is a chronic inflammatory disease that destroys the supporting structures of the teeth — the gums, periodontal ligament, and alveolar bone. It is the sixth most common complication of diabetes.

Stages

  • Gingivitis: The earliest stage — red, swollen gums that bleed easily during brushing or flossing. Reversible with good oral hygiene and professional cleaning.
  • Early periodontitis: Inflammation extends deeper; early bone loss begins. Pockets form between teeth and gums (4-5 mm).
  • Moderate periodontitis: Significant bone loss; deeper pockets (5-7 mm); teeth may begin to loosen.
  • Advanced periodontitis: Severe bone destruction; deep pockets (>7 mm); tooth mobility and migration; tooth loss.

Symptoms to Watch For

  • Gums that bleed during brushing, flossing, or eating.
  • Red, swollen, or tender gums.
  • Gums that have pulled away from the teeth (recession).
  • Persistent bad breath (halitosis) or bad taste in the mouth.
  • Loose teeth or teeth that have shifted position.
  • Changes in bite alignment or denture fit.
  • Pus between teeth and gums.

💧 Dry Mouth (Xerostomia)

Reduced saliva production is common in diabetes, caused by hyperglycemia, dehydration, autonomic neuropathy, and several diabetes medications.

  • Saliva is essential for neutralizing acids, washing away food particles, and controlling bacterial growth.
  • Dry mouth increases the risk of dental cavities, gum disease, and oral infections.
  • Symptoms: persistent dryness, difficulty swallowing or speaking, cracked lips, burning sensation, altered taste.

Management

  • Stay well hydrated — sip water throughout the day.
  • Chew sugar-free gum or suck sugar-free candy to stimulate saliva production.
  • Use alcohol-free mouthwash (alcohol worsens dryness).
  • Consider saliva substitutes or artificial saliva products (gels, sprays).
  • Use a humidifier at night.
  • Avoid caffeine, alcohol, and tobacco, which all worsen dry mouth.
  • If severe, discuss with your doctor — medications like pilocarpine may help.

🦫 Oral Candidiasis (Thrush)

A fungal infection caused by Candida species, which thrives in the high-glucose, low-saliva environment of the diabetic mouth.

  • Appears as white, creamy patches on the tongue, inner cheeks, or palate that can be wiped off, leaving red or bleeding tissue underneath.
  • Can also present as redness and soreness without white patches (erythematous candidiasis).
  • Angular cheilitis (cracking at the corners of the mouth) is often related to Candida.
  • More common with poor glycemic control, denture use, dry mouth, antibiotic use, or inhaled corticosteroids.
  • Treatment: topical antifungals (nystatin suspension, clotrimazole troches) or systemic fluconazole for severe/recurrent cases.
  • Prevention: good glucose control, proper denture hygiene, and treating dry mouth.

👁 Other Oral Manifestations

  • Burning mouth syndrome: A burning sensation in the tongue or oral mucosa, possibly related to neuropathy or dry mouth.
  • Delayed wound healing: Oral surgical wounds (extractions, biopsies) heal more slowly in poorly controlled diabetes.
  • Increased cavity risk: High salivary glucose feeds bacteria; reduced saliva decreases natural protection.
  • Lichen planus: Slightly more prevalent in diabetes; presents as white, lacy patches or painful erosions.
  • Taste disturbances: Altered taste perception (dysgeusia) may occur with neuropathy or dry mouth.

Daily Home Care

  • Brush teeth at least twice daily with a soft-bristled toothbrush and fluoride toothpaste.
  • Replace your toothbrush every 3 months or when bristles become frayed.
  • Floss at least once daily — cleans the 35% of tooth surfaces that brushing misses.
  • Consider an electric toothbrush — studies show they are more effective at plaque removal.
  • Use an antimicrobial or fluoride mouthwash (alcohol-free if you have dry mouth).
  • Clean dentures daily if applicable; remove them at night.

Professional Care

  • Dental cleanings every 6 months (or more frequently if periodontitis is present — every 3-4 months).
  • Comprehensive periodontal examination at least annually.
  • Full-mouth X-rays every 1-2 years to assess bone levels.
  • Deep cleaning (scaling and root planing) if pockets are present.

📋 Inform Your Dentist About Your Diabetes

  • Tell your dentist that you have diabetes, your current HbA1c, and all medications you take.
  • Bring a list of your medications to every dental appointment (especially anticoagulants, antiplatelet agents, and bisphosphonates).
  • Schedule dental procedures in the morning when blood glucose tends to be most stable.
  • Eat normally and take your diabetes medications before the appointment (do not skip meals).
  • Bring glucose tablets or a snack in case of hypoglycemia during or after the procedure.
  • Inform your dentist if your blood glucose has been poorly controlled recently — elective procedures may need to be postponed.
  • Antibiotics may be needed before or after invasive procedures if glucose control is poor or if you have heart valve disease.
Good communication between your diabetes care team and your dentist improves outcomes for both conditions. Ask your dentist to send updates to your primary physician or endocrinologist.
