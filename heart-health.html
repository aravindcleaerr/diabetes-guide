Heart Health & Cardiovascular Disease

Cardiovascular disease is the leading cause of death in people with diabetes. Understanding risk factors and protective strategies is essential.

💔 The Diabetes-Heart Connection

Cardiovascular disease (CVD) accounts for approximately 50-80% of deaths in people with diabetes. Adults with Type 2 diabetes have a 2-4 times higher risk of heart attack and stroke compared to people without diabetes. This excess risk persists even after adjusting for traditional risk factors like smoking and cholesterol.

Diabetes accelerates atherosclerosis through multiple mechanisms: chronic hyperglycemia, insulin resistance, dyslipidemia (high triglycerides, low HDL, small dense LDL), hypertension, chronic inflammation, oxidative stress, and endothelial dysfunction.

Many people with diabetes experience "silent" heart attacks with minimal or atypical symptoms (fatigue, shortness of breath, nausea) rather than classic chest pain. Maintain a high index of suspicion and report any unusual symptoms.

💉 Blood Pressure Management

Hypertension is extremely common in diabetes and is a major driver of both macrovascular and microvascular complications.

  • Target: <130/80 mmHg for most adults with diabetes (ADA/AHA recommendation).
  • First-line medications: ACE inhibitors (e.g., ramipril, enalapril) or ARBs (e.g., losartan, telmisartan) — preferred because they also protect the kidneys.
  • Additional agents as needed: calcium channel blockers (amlodipine), thiazide diuretics.
  • Home blood pressure monitoring is recommended for accurate assessment.
  • Lifestyle measures: sodium restriction (<2,300 mg/day, ideally <1,500 mg), DASH diet, regular exercise, weight loss, moderation of alcohol.
Do not combine ACE inhibitors and ARBs together — this increases the risk of hyperkalemia and renal impairment without additional benefit.

📉 Lipid Management

Targets

  • LDL cholesterol: <100 mg/dL for most adults with diabetes; <70 mg/dL (or 50% reduction) for those with established CVD or high risk.
  • Triglycerides: <150 mg/dL.
  • HDL: >40 mg/dL (or >50 mg/dL depending on individual risk factors).
  • Non-HDL cholesterol: <130 mg/dL (or <100 mg/dL for very high risk).

Statin Therapy

  • Moderate-intensity statin (e.g., atorvastatin 10-20 mg, rosuvastatin 5-10 mg): recommended for all adults with diabetes aged 40-75 without established CVD.
  • High-intensity statin (e.g., atorvastatin 40-80 mg, rosuvastatin 20-40 mg): recommended for those with established CVD or multiple risk factors.
  • For ages 20-39, consider a statin if additional risk factors are present.
  • If LDL remains above target on maximum tolerated statin, add ezetimibe or a PCSK9 inhibitor.
Statins are one of the most evidence-backed interventions in diabetes. Side effects (muscle aches) are uncommon. Do not stop your statin without discussing with your doctor — the cardiovascular benefits far outweigh the risks for most people.

💊 Antiplatelet Therapy

  • Secondary prevention: Low-dose aspirin (75-162 mg/day) is recommended for all people with diabetes and established CVD (prior heart attack, stroke, or PAD).
  • Primary prevention: Consider aspirin for those with diabetes at higher CVD risk (10-year ASCVD risk >10%) after discussing bleeding risk. Not routinely recommended for low-risk individuals.
  • Clopidogrel is an alternative for those who are aspirin-intolerant.
  • Dual antiplatelet therapy (aspirin + clopidogrel) after acute coronary syndrome or stent placement, typically for 12 months.

💚 ACE Inhibitors and ARBs

Beyond blood pressure control, these agents provide cardiovascular and renal protection:

  • Reduce left ventricular hypertrophy and heart failure progression.
  • Protect against diabetic nephropathy by reducing intraglomerular pressure.
  • Recommended as first-line antihypertensive in diabetes, especially with albuminuria.
  • Monitor serum creatinine and potassium within 1-2 weeks of initiation or dose change.
  • Contraindicated in pregnancy.

😊 Cardiovascular Benefits of Newer Diabetes Medications

SGLT2 Inhibitors (empagliflozin, canagliflozin, dapagliflozin)

  • Demonstrated reduction in major adverse cardiovascular events (MACE) in landmark trials (EMPA-REG OUTCOME, CANVAS, DECLARE-TIMI 58).
  • Significant reduction in heart failure hospitalization (30-35% relative risk reduction).
  • Renal protective effects (slow progression of CKD).
  • Benefits appear to be partly independent of glucose lowering.
  • Now recommended by guidelines for people with diabetes and established CVD, heart failure, or CKD regardless of HbA1c.

GLP-1 Receptor Agonists (liraglutide, semaglutide, dulaglutide)

  • Reduce MACE (cardiovascular death, non-fatal MI, non-fatal stroke) in trials (LEADER, SUSTAIN-6, REWIND).
  • Promote weight loss (3-7 kg on average), which provides additional metabolic benefits.
  • Reduce blood pressure and improve lipid profile.
  • Recommended for people with diabetes and established atherosclerotic CVD or high CVD risk.
Current ADA/EASD guidelines recommend adding an SGLT2 inhibitor or GLP-1 RA with proven cardiovascular benefit for people with Type 2 diabetes who have established CVD, heart failure, or CKD — independent of HbA1c level or metformin use.

🏃 Lifestyle Modifications for Heart Health

  • Physical activity: At least 150 minutes/week of moderate-intensity aerobic exercise (brisk walking, cycling, swimming) plus 2-3 sessions of resistance training.
  • Diet: Mediterranean or DASH dietary pattern — rich in vegetables, fruits, whole grains, legumes, nuts, fish, and olive oil; limit saturated fat, trans fat, sodium, and added sugars.
  • Weight management: Even 5-10% weight loss significantly improves cardiovascular risk factors.
  • Smoking cessation: The single most impactful lifestyle change for reducing CVD risk. Seek support through counseling and pharmacotherapy (varenicline, NRT) if needed.
  • Alcohol: If consumed, limit to 1–2 drinks/day depending on body size. Avoid binge drinking.
  • Stress management: Chronic stress elevates cortisol and blood glucose. Consider mindfulness, therapy, and regular sleep.

💔 Heart Failure in Diabetes

People with diabetes are at 2-5 times higher risk of heart failure, even without coronary artery disease. This includes heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF) and heart failure with preserved ejection fraction (HFpEF).

  • Symptoms: shortness of breath, fatigue, swelling in legs/ankles, rapid weight gain.
  • SGLT2 inhibitors are now a cornerstone of heart failure treatment in people with diabetes (and even without diabetes).
  • Avoid thiazolidinediones (pioglitazone, rosiglitazone) in patients with heart failure — they cause fluid retention.
  • Metformin is safe in stable heart failure but should be held during acute decompensation.

🚨 When to Seek Urgent Care

Call emergency services immediately if you experience:
  • Chest pain, pressure, or tightness lasting more than a few minutes
  • Pain radiating to the arm, jaw, neck, or back
  • Sudden shortness of breath
  • Sudden weakness or numbness on one side of the body
  • Sudden difficulty speaking or understanding speech
  • Sudden severe headache with no known cause
  • Loss of consciousness
