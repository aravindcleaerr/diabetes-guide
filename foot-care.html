Foot Care

Diabetes-related foot problems are a leading cause of hospitalization and amputation. Most are preventable with daily care, proper footwear, and early intervention.

🦶 Why Foot Care Matters

Peripheral neuropathy (loss of sensation), peripheral arterial disease (reduced blood flow), and impaired immune function create a "perfect storm" in the diabetic foot. Minor injuries that would heal quickly in a healthy person can become non-healing ulcers, deep infections, and ultimately lead to amputation.

  • Diabetes accounts for more than 50% of all non-traumatic lower-limb amputations.
  • A foot ulcer precedes 85% of diabetes-related amputations.
  • 5-year mortality after a diabetes-related amputation exceeds 50% — worse than many cancers.
  • Up to 85% of amputations are potentially preventable with proper foot care and early intervention.

🔎 Daily Foot Inspection Routine

Make foot inspection a daily habit, ideally at the same time each day (e.g., before bed or after bathing).

  • Look at the tops, bottoms, sides, and between all toes of both feet
  • Use a mirror or ask a family member to help inspect the soles
  • Check for cuts, blisters, cracks, redness, swelling, or warmth
  • Check for calluses, corns, bruises, color changes, drainage, or odor
  • Feel for temperature differences between the two feet
  • Check that toenails are intact and not ingrown
  • Wash feet with lukewarm water and mild soap
  • Dry thoroughly, especially between toes
  • Apply moisturizer (not between toes)
  • Inspect shoes before wearing — shake out and feel inside
Make it part of your routine — keep a small mirror by your bed or chair. Even if your feet feel fine, look at them every day. Neuropathy means you cannot rely on pain to alert you to problems.

💧 Washing and Moisturizing

  • Wash feet daily with lukewarm water and mild soap. Test water temperature with your elbow or a thermometer — never your feet (you may not feel if it is too hot).
  • Do not soak feet for prolonged periods — this softens the skin excessively and increases infection risk.
  • Dry feet thoroughly, especially between the toes (moisture promotes fungal infections).
  • Apply a fragrance-free moisturizing lotion or cream to the tops and bottoms of feet to prevent drying and cracking.
  • Do not apply moisturizer between the toes (traps moisture, promotes fungal growth).

✂ Proper Nail Care

  • Cut toenails straight across — do not round the corners (prevents ingrown toenails).
  • File edges gently with an emery board to smooth sharp corners.
  • Do not cut nails too short — leave a small margin of white.
  • Cut nails after bathing when they are softer.
  • If you have thick nails, poor vision, neuropathy, or poor circulation, have a podiatrist cut your nails.
  • Never use sharp instruments (razor blades, scissors) to remove calluses or corns.
  • Do not use chemical corn/callus removers — they can burn diabetic skin.

👞 Footwear Selection

  • Wide toe box: Shoes should have ample room for toes without crowding or friction.
  • Proper fit: Shop for shoes in the afternoon (feet swell during the day). Have both feet measured — sizes may differ.
  • Break in new shoes gradually: Wear for 1-2 hours at a time initially and check feet for red spots or blisters.
  • Closed-toe shoes: Protect feet from injury. Avoid sandals, flip-flops, and pointed shoes.
  • No bare feet — ever: Not at home, not at the beach, not at the pool. Always wear shoes or slippers.
  • Inspect shoes before wearing: Shake out shoes and feel inside for pebbles, rough seams, or foreign objects.
  • Custom orthotic insoles: Prescribed therapeutic footwear and custom insoles reduce ulcer recurrence by 25-50%. Covered by insurance in many countries for high-risk patients.
  • Avoid high heels and shoes with thin soles (inadequate cushioning and pressure redistribution).

Diabetic Socks

  • Seamless construction to prevent friction and pressure points.
  • Moisture-wicking materials (avoid 100% cotton).
  • Non-constricting tops (no tight elastic bands that impair circulation).
  • Light padding on the sole for cushioning.
  • Change socks daily; avoid wearing damp socks.

⚠ Charcot Foot (Charcot Neuroarthropathy)

A serious complication where neuropathy leads to progressive weakening, fracture, and dislocation of bones and joints in the foot, often without significant pain. If untreated, the foot collapses into a "rocker bottom" deformity.

  • Early signs: Unilateral swelling, redness, and warmth in the foot (often mistaken for infection or gout).
  • Diagnosis: Clinical suspicion is key. X-rays may initially be normal; MRI is more sensitive. Compare skin temperature between both feet (difference of >2°C is significant).
  • Treatment: Immediate offloading (total contact cast or removable walker) for 3-6 months until the acute phase resolves. Weight-bearing on an active Charcot foot causes progressive destruction.
  • Long-term: Custom molded shoes and orthotics. Surgery may be needed for unstable deformities.
A red, hot, swollen foot in a person with diabetic neuropathy is Charcot foot until proven otherwise. This is an emergency — continued walking on an active Charcot foot can destroy the foot's architecture in weeks.

🫁 Peripheral Arterial Disease (PAD)

Reduced blood flow to the feet from atherosclerosis of the leg arteries. PAD combined with neuropathy dramatically increases amputation risk.

  • Symptoms: Claudication (calf pain with walking that resolves with rest), rest pain (severe — suggests critical limb ischemia), cold feet, slow-healing wounds, absent pulses.
  • Screening: Palpate dorsalis pedis and posterior tibial pulses at every visit. Ankle-brachial index (ABI) if symptoms or absent pulses (normal 1.0-1.3; <0.9 indicates PAD).
  • Treatment: Smoking cessation (critical), antiplatelet therapy, statins, supervised exercise programs, revascularization (angioplasty/stent or bypass surgery) for critical limb ischemia.

🩺 When to See a Podiatrist

  • At least annually for comprehensive foot risk assessment (more frequently if high risk).
  • For routine nail care if you have neuropathy, PAD, poor vision, or limited mobility.
  • Immediately for any wound, ulcer, infection, or color change.
  • For callus debridement — never remove calluses yourself.
  • For footwear evaluation and custom orthotic prescription.
  • After any previous ulcer or amputation — you are at very high risk for recurrence.

🩹 Wound Care Basics

  • Clean the wound gently with saline or clean water. Avoid hydrogen peroxide or iodine (they damage healing tissue).
  • Apply a sterile dressing and keep the wound moist (moist wound healing is faster than dry).
  • Offloading is critical: Take pressure off the affected area. Do not walk on a foot ulcer — use crutches, a wheelchair, or a prescribed offloading device (total contact cast, removable cast walker).
  • Monitor for signs of infection: increasing redness, swelling, warmth, pain, drainage, fever, or worsening blood glucose.
  • Seek medical attention for any wound that does not show improvement within 1-2 weeks despite proper care.
Diabetic foot ulcers require a multidisciplinary approach: endocrinologist (glucose control), podiatrist (wound care, offloading), vascular surgeon (blood flow assessment), infectious disease specialist (if infected), and orthotist (therapeutic footwear).
