Exercise & Fitness

How physical activity improves blood sugar control and overall health in diabetes

🏃 ADA Exercise Recommendations

The American Diabetes Association (ADA) and the American College of Sports Medicine recommend:

  • Aerobic exercise: At least 150 minutes per week of moderate-intensity activity (e.g., brisk walking), spread over at least 3 days, with no more than 2 consecutive days without exercise.
  • Resistance training: At least 2–3 sessions per week on non-consecutive days, targeting all major muscle groups.
  • Reduce sedentary time: Break up prolonged sitting every 30 minutes with light activity (standing, walking, stretching).
  • Flexibility and balance: 2–3 times per week, especially for older adults (reduces fall risk).
Even small amounts of physical activity are beneficial. If 150 minutes feels daunting, start with 10-minute walks after meals and build up gradually.

Resistance / Strength Training

Builds muscle mass, which acts as a glucose sink, improving insulin sensitivity even at rest. Muscle is the primary site for glucose disposal in the body.

  • Options: Free weights, resistance bands, bodyweight exercises (push-ups, squats, lunges), gym machines.
  • Start with: 1–2 sets of 10–15 repetitions per exercise, targeting 8–10 major muscle groups.
  • Progress to: 3 sets of 8–12 reps with heavier weights over time.
  • Studies show resistance training can lower HbA1c by 0.3–0.6%, comparable to adding a diabetes medication.

Yoga

Combines physical postures, breathing exercises, and meditation. Particularly beneficial for stress reduction, flexibility, and moderate glucose lowering.

High-Intensity Interval Training (HIIT)

Alternating short bursts of intense exercise with rest periods. Time-efficient (20–30 minutes) and effective for improving insulin sensitivity. Should be attempted only after building a base fitness level.

🩸 Blood Sugar and Exercise

Exercise affects blood sugar in complex ways. Understanding these patterns helps you exercise safely.

How Exercise Lowers Blood Sugar

  • Muscles take up glucose from the blood during activity — this effect can last 24–72 hours.
  • Exercise improves insulin sensitivity, meaning your cells respond better to insulin.
  • Regular exercise can lower HbA1c by 0.5–0.7% on average.

Check Blood Sugar Before, During, and After Exercise

Blood Sugar LevelAction
<100 mg/dL (5.6 mmol/L)Eat a small carb snack (15–20 g) before exercising. Examples: a banana, 4 glucose tablets, a granola bar.
100–250 mg/dLSafe to exercise. This is the ideal range to begin.
>250 mg/dL (13.9 mmol/L)Check for ketones (urine or blood). If ketones are positive, do NOT exercise — exercise can worsen ketoacidosis. If no ketones, light exercise may be acceptable.
>300 mg/dLDo not exercise regardless of ketone status. Consult your doctor.

During Exercise

  • For exercise lasting >60 minutes, check glucose every 30 minutes.
  • Keep fast-acting glucose (glucose tablets, juice box) accessible.
  • If glucose drops below 70 mg/dL, stop exercising, consume 15 g fast carbs, wait 15 minutes, recheck (the “rule of 15”).

After Exercise

  • Blood sugar can continue to drop for up to 24 hours post-exercise (delayed hypoglycemia), especially after intense or prolonged activity.
  • Check blood sugar after exercise and again before bed if you exercised in the evening.
  • Have a balanced snack containing carbs + protein within 30–60 minutes if needed.

⚠️ Exercise Precautions

Important safety guidelines:
  • Always carry fast-acting glucose (tablets, candy, juice) during exercise.
  • Wear a medical ID bracelet or carry identification indicating you have diabetes.
  • Stay hydrated — dehydration can raise blood sugar.
  • Wear proper footwear; inspect feet before and after exercise (especially if you have neuropathy).
  • Avoid exercising in extreme heat or cold.
  • If you take insulin or sulfonylureas, discuss exercise-related dose adjustments with your doctor.
  • Stop exercising immediately if you feel dizzy, shaky, confused, nauseous, or have chest pain.

Special Considerations

  • Retinopathy (proliferative): Avoid heavy lifting, straining, and high-impact activities that raise blood pressure sharply — risk of vitreous hemorrhage.
  • Peripheral neuropathy: Avoid high-impact activities (running on hard surfaces, jumping). Choose swimming, cycling, or chair exercises instead.
  • Autonomic neuropathy: Heart rate response may be blunted; use perceived exertion rather than heart rate to gauge intensity.
  • Nephropathy: Light to moderate exercise is safe and beneficial; avoid excessively strenuous activity.

⏰ Best Times to Exercise

  • After meals (1–2 hours post-meal): This is often the best time because it helps blunt post-meal glucose spikes. A 15–30 minute walk after lunch or dinner can lower post-meal glucose by 20–40 mg/dL.
  • Morning: Good for consistency and habit-building. Some people experience a “dawn phenomenon” (high morning glucose) that responds well to morning exercise.
  • Evening: Fine for most people, but monitor for delayed overnight hypoglycemia if on insulin.
  • Avoid: Exercising during peak insulin action (check with your doctor about your medication timing).
The best time to exercise is the time you will actually do it consistently. Regularity matters more than timing.

🏋️ Resistance Training — Why It Matters

Resistance training deserves special emphasis because it is often underutilised by people with diabetes, yet it provides unique benefits:

  • Increases muscle mass: Muscle is the largest insulin-sensitive tissue. More muscle means better glucose disposal.
  • Improves insulin sensitivity: Independent of weight loss or aerobic fitness.
  • Prevents sarcopenia: Age-related muscle loss accelerates in diabetes. Resistance training counteracts this.
  • Improves bone density: Reduces fracture risk, which is elevated in diabetes.
  • Boosts metabolism: Muscle burns more calories at rest than fat tissue.
  • Lowers HbA1c: Meta-analyses show 0.3–0.6% reduction with regular resistance training.

🧘 Yoga Asanas for Diabetes

Several yoga poses have been studied for their effects on blood glucose, insulin sensitivity, and stress hormones (cortisol). Practice on an empty stomach or 2 hours after a meal.

Surya Namaskar (Sun Salutation)

A flowing sequence of 12 poses that combines stretching, forward and backward bends, and breathing. 6–12 rounds provide a full-body workout.

  • Improves cardiovascular fitness and flexibility.
  • Studies show regular practice reduces fasting glucose and improves HbA1c.
  • Start with 4 rounds and increase gradually.

Mandukasana (Frog Pose)

Sit in vajrasana (kneeling position), place fists on the abdomen (at the navel), exhale and bend forward pressing fists into the abdomen. Hold for 15–30 seconds.

  • Compresses the pancreas and abdominal organs, believed to stimulate insulin secretion.
  • Improves digestion.
  • Repeat 3–5 times.

Paschimottanasana (Seated Forward Bend)

Sit with legs extended, inhale and raise arms, exhale and fold forward from the hips reaching for the toes. Hold for 30–60 seconds.

  • Stimulates abdominal organs including the pancreas.
  • Calms the nervous system; reduces stress hormones.
  • Improves hamstring flexibility.

🚀 Getting Started — Practical Tips

  • Get medical clearance before starting a new exercise program, especially if you have complications or have been sedentary.
  • Start slow — even 10 minutes of walking 3 times a day counts toward your 150-minute goal.
  • Find activities you enjoy — you are more likely to stick with something fun.
  • Exercise with a buddy for motivation and safety.
  • Keep an exercise log alongside your blood sugar readings to see patterns.
  • Set realistic, incremental goals (e.g., “walk 20 minutes daily this week; increase to 30 minutes next week”).
