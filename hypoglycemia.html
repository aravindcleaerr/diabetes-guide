Hypoglycemia (Low Blood Sugar)

Recognising, treating, and preventing dangerously low blood glucose levels

EMERGENCY WARNING: Severe hypoglycemia (blood sugar below 54 mg/dL) is a medical emergency that can cause seizures, loss of consciousness, and death. If someone is unconscious or unable to swallow, call 112 (India) / emergency services immediately. Do NOT attempt to give food or liquid by mouth to an unconscious person.

⚠️ What Is Hypoglycemia?

Hypoglycemia occurs when blood glucose falls below 70 mg/dL (3.9 mmol/L). It is one of the most common and potentially dangerous acute complications of diabetes treatment, particularly in people using insulin or insulin secretagogues (sulfonylureas, meglitinides).

ClassificationBlood Glucose LevelClinical Significance
Level 1 (Alert)< 70 mg/dL (3.9 mmol/L)Glucose low enough to require treatment with fast-acting carbohydrate
Level 2 (Clinically significant)< 54 mg/dL (3.0 mmol/L)Sufficiently low to indicate serious, clinically important hypoglycemia
Level 3 (Severe)No specific thresholdHypoglycemia associated with severe cognitive impairment requiring external assistance for recovery

🔍 Causes

  • Too much insulin or insulin secretagogues: Incorrect dosing, stacking insulin doses, or using the wrong insulin type (e.g., rapid-acting instead of long-acting).
  • Missed or delayed meals: Skipping meals or eating significantly less carbohydrate than planned after taking insulin or sulfonylureas.
  • Excess physical activity: Exercise increases glucose uptake by muscles independently of insulin; unplanned or intense exercise without adjusting food intake or insulin dose can cause glucose to drop rapidly. Effects can persist for up to 24 hours post-exercise.
  • Alcohol consumption: Alcohol inhibits hepatic gluconeogenesis, impairing the liver's ability to release glucose. Risk is highest when drinking on an empty stomach or several hours after drinking.
  • Improved insulin sensitivity: Weight loss, increased fitness, or resolution of an illness or stress state can increase insulin sensitivity, making previous doses excessive.
  • Renal impairment: Reduced kidney function prolongs insulin clearance, increasing hypoglycemia risk.
  • Drug interactions: Certain medications (e.g., beta-blockers, quinolones, quinine) can potentiate hypoglycemia or mask its symptoms.

🚨 Symptoms by Severity

Mild (Adrenergic / Autonomic Symptoms)

Triggered by the sympathetic nervous system response to falling glucose:

  • Trembling / shaking
  • Sweating (diaphoresis)
  • Hunger (often sudden and intense)
  • Heart palpitations / rapid heartbeat
  • Anxiety or nervousness
  • Tingling or numbness around the lips and fingertips
  • Pallor (pale skin)

Moderate (Neuroglycopenic Symptoms)

Caused by insufficient glucose supply to the brain:

  • Confusion and difficulty concentrating
  • Blurred or double vision
  • Slurred speech
  • Poor coordination and unsteady gait
  • Irritability or unusual mood changes
  • Drowsiness and fatigue
  • Headache

Severe

Medical emergency requiring immediate external assistance:

  • Seizures / convulsions
  • Loss of consciousness
  • Inability to swallow safely
  • Coma (if untreated)
  • In rare cases, death or permanent brain injury if prolonged
Warning: Severe hypoglycemia is a medical emergency. If a person is unconscious or seizing, NEVER attempt to give food or liquids by mouth — this poses a serious aspiration risk. Administer glucagon and call emergency services immediately.

🎯 Treatment: The Rule of 15

The Rule of 15 is the standard first-aid protocol for conscious individuals experiencing hypoglycemia:

🚨 Act Fast: Untreated hypoglycemia can lead to seizures, loss of consciousness, and brain injury within minutes. Do not delay treatment.

Step 1: Check
Confirm glucose < 70 mg/dL
🍬
Step 2: Eat 15 g
Fast-acting carbs (glucose tabs, juice, soda)
⏳
Step 3: Wait 15 min
Recheck blood glucose
🔄
Step 4: Repeat
If still < 70, repeat Step 2

15 g Fast-Acting Carbohydrate Options

  • 3–4 glucose tablets
  • 4 oz (120 mL) of fruit juice (not sugar-free)
  • 4 oz of regular soda (not diet)
  • 1 tablespoon of honey or sugar
  • Hard sweets equivalent to 15 g of sugar

Once glucose is above 70 mg/dL, eat a balanced snack or meal containing protein and complex carbohydrate (e.g., peanut butter crackers, cheese and wholegrain bread) to prevent recurrence.

Tip: Do not use chocolate, ice cream, or other high-fat foods to treat hypoglycemia. Fat slows glucose absorption and delays recovery. Use pure, fast-acting glucose sources.

💦 Glucagon Emergency Kit

Glucagon is a hormone that stimulates the liver to release stored glycogen as glucose. It is the primary rescue treatment for severe hypoglycemia when the person cannot take carbohydrate by mouth.

When to Use Glucagon

  • The person is unconscious or unable to swallow.
  • The person is having a seizure.
  • The person is too confused or combative to safely consume oral carbohydrate.

After Administering Glucagon

  • Place the person in the recovery position (on their side) to prevent aspiration if they vomit (nausea is common).
  • Call emergency services (911 / 112 / local equivalent).
  • Once conscious, give oral carbohydrate as soon as they can swallow safely.
  • Glucagon may not work effectively in people with depleted glycogen stores (e.g., prolonged fasting, heavy alcohol use, liver disease).
Note: Everyone who uses insulin and their close contacts (family, friends, coworkers, teachers) should know where the glucagon kit is stored and how to use it. Practice with a trainer device regularly.

🛡️ Prevention Strategies

  • Regular blood glucose monitoring: Use a glucose meter or continuous glucose monitor (CGM). CGMs with low-glucose alerts are particularly valuable.
  • Consistent meal timing: Eat meals and snacks at regular intervals. Do not skip meals, especially after taking insulin or sulfonylureas.
  • Carbohydrate awareness: Match insulin doses to carbohydrate intake. Learn carbohydrate counting if on a flexible insulin regimen.
  • Exercise planning: Check glucose before, during, and after exercise. Have fast-acting carbohydrate available. Consider reducing insulin dose or eating an extra snack before planned activity.
  • Alcohol precautions: Never drink on an empty stomach. Monitor glucose more frequently for up to 24 hours after drinking. Eat a carbohydrate-containing snack with alcohol.
  • Medication review: Work with your healthcare team to identify and adjust high-risk medications. Consider switching from sulfonylureas to agents with lower hypoglycemia risk (e.g., DPP-4 inhibitors, GLP-1 RAs, SGLT2i).
  • Always carry fast-acting glucose: Keep glucose tablets, juice, or sweets accessible at all times — in a pocket, bag, car, bedside table, and desk.
  • Medical identification: Wear a medical ID bracelet or necklace indicating diabetes and insulin use.

🌙 Nocturnal Hypoglycemia

Nocturnal hypoglycemia occurs during sleep (typically between 2:00 AM and 4:00 AM) and is particularly dangerous because the person is unaware of symptoms.

Warning Signs

  • Night sweats (waking with damp sheets or clothing)
  • Restless sleep or nightmares
  • Waking with a headache
  • Feeling unusually tired or irritable in the morning
  • Unexplained high morning glucose (rebound hyperglycemia / Somogyi effect)

Prevention

  • Check blood glucose at bedtime. A reading below 120 mg/dL may warrant a bedtime snack (e.g., complex carbohydrate + protein).
  • Use a CGM with low-glucose alarms — this is the single most effective tool for detecting nocturnal lows.
  • Consider a closed-loop insulin pump system with predictive low-glucose suspend (PLGS) technology.
  • Discuss basal insulin timing, type, and dose with your endocrinologist. Switching from NPH to a long-acting analogue (glargine, degludec) reduces nocturnal hypoglycemia risk.
  • Avoid vigorous exercise late in the evening without compensatory carbohydrate intake.
Tip: If you experience recurrent nocturnal lows, ask your diabetes team about adjusting your basal insulin dose or switching to a pump with predictive low-glucose suspend capability.

👁️ Hypoglycemia Unawareness

Hypoglycemia unawareness is a condition in which the body's normal adrenergic (autonomic) warning symptoms of low blood glucose are diminished or absent. The person may progress directly from feeling fine to severe neuroglycopenic symptoms (confusion, seizure, unconsciousness) without the usual early warning signs of shaking, sweating, and hunger.

Who Is at Risk?

  • People with long-standing Type 1 diabetes (especially > 10 years)
  • Those with frequent recurrent hypoglycemia (the brain adapts to low glucose, shifting its counter-regulatory response to lower thresholds)
  • Patients with very tight glycaemic targets (HbA1c < 6.0%)
  • Individuals with autonomic neuropathy
  • Elderly patients

Restoring Awareness

  • Strict hypoglycemia avoidance: Maintaining glucose above 70 mg/dL for 2–3 weeks can restore counter-regulatory hormone responses and symptom awareness in many patients. This may require temporarily relaxing HbA1c targets.
  • Continuous glucose monitoring: CGMs with alarms are essential for safety in patients with unawareness.
  • Closed-loop pump systems: Automated insulin delivery with PLGS or full closed-loop algorithms significantly reduces hypoglycemia frequency and can help restore awareness over time.
  • Structured education programmes: Programmes such as DAFNE (Dose Adjustment for Normal Eating) and BGAT (Blood Glucose Awareness Training) teach patients to recognise subtle cues and improve self-management.
Warning: Hypoglycemia unawareness significantly increases the risk of severe hypoglycemia by six-fold. If you have lost your warning symptoms, inform your diabetes team immediately. Driving restrictions may apply — check local regulations.
