Understanding Diabetes

A comprehensive overview of diabetes mellitus, its types, causes, and impact on global health

Content last reviewed: January 2026 | AI-assisted content

🧬 What Is Diabetes?

Diabetes mellitus is a group of chronic metabolic diseases characterized by persistently elevated blood glucose (blood sugar) levels. This occurs either because the pancreas does not produce enough insulin, or because the body's cells do not respond properly to the insulin that is produced — a condition known as insulin resistance.

Insulin is a peptide hormone produced by the beta cells of the islets of Langerhans in the pancreas. Its primary role is to facilitate the uptake of glucose from the bloodstream into cells, where it is used for energy. When this process fails, glucose accumulates in the blood, leading to hyperglycemia and a cascade of complications affecting nearly every organ system.

Key Fact: According to the International Diabetes Federation (IDF), approximately 537 million adults (aged 20–79) worldwide were living with diabetes in 2021. This number is projected to reach 783 million by 2045 if current trends continue.

📊 Types of Diabetes

While the term "diabetes" is often used broadly, there are several distinct types, each with different causes, risk profiles, and management strategies:

  • Type 1 Diabetes (T1D): An autoimmune condition in which the immune system destroys insulin-producing beta cells. Accounts for approximately 5–10% of all diabetes cases. Requires exogenous insulin from the time of diagnosis.
  • Type 2 Diabetes (T2D): Characterized by insulin resistance and a progressive decline in beta-cell function. Represents 90–95% of all diabetes cases. Strongly associated with obesity, sedentary lifestyle, and genetic predisposition.
  • Prediabetes: A metabolic state where blood glucose levels are higher than normal but not yet high enough to be classified as Type 2 diabetes. Prediabetes is reversible with lifestyle interventions.
  • Gestational Diabetes Mellitus (GDM): Diabetes first recognized during pregnancy (usually in the second or third trimester). It increases the risk of complications for both the pregnant person and the child and raises the lifetime risk of developing T2D.
  • Other Specific Types: Includes monogenic diabetes (MODY — Maturity-Onset Diabetes of the Young), cystic fibrosis-related diabetes, steroid-induced diabetes, and diabetes secondary to pancreatitis or other endocrine disorders.

🦴 How Diabetes Affects the Body

The Role of the Pancreas and Insulin

The pancreas is a glandular organ located behind the stomach. It has both exocrine functions (producing digestive enzymes) and endocrine functions (producing hormones). The endocrine portion — the islets of Langerhans — contains several cell types:

  • Beta cells: Produce insulin, which lowers blood glucose.
  • Alpha cells: Produce glucagon, which raises blood glucose.
  • Delta cells: Produce somatostatin, which modulates the release of both insulin and glucagon.

In a healthy individual, when blood glucose rises after a meal, beta cells release insulin. Insulin signals muscle, fat, and liver cells to absorb glucose. In diabetes, this finely tuned system breaks down.

Insulin Resistance

In Type 2 diabetes and prediabetes, cells in the muscles, fat tissue, and liver become less responsive to insulin. The pancreas compensates by producing more insulin (hyperinsulinemia), but over time, beta cells become exhausted and cannot keep up with demand, leading to rising blood glucose levels.

Autoimmune Destruction

In Type 1 diabetes, the immune system mistakenly targets and destroys beta cells. Specific autoantibodies — including GAD65 antibodies, IA-2 antibodies, insulin autoantibodies (IAA), and ZnT8 antibodies — can be detected in the blood, often years before symptoms appear.

Systemic Complications

Chronic hyperglycemia damages blood vessels and nerves throughout the body, leading to:

  • Microvascular complications: Retinopathy (eyes), nephropathy (kidneys), neuropathy (nerves)
  • Macrovascular complications: Coronary artery disease, stroke, peripheral arterial disease
  • Other: Increased susceptibility to infections, delayed wound healing, sexual dysfunction, cognitive decline, depression
Warning: Uncontrolled diabetes is the leading cause of adult-onset blindness, end-stage kidney disease, and non-traumatic lower-limb amputations in many countries. Early detection and consistent management are critical to preventing these outcomes.

⚠️ Risk Factors

Non-Modifiable Risk Factors

  • Family history: Having a first-degree relative with diabetes significantly increases risk.
  • Age: Risk increases with age, particularly after age 45 for T2D.
  • Ethnicity: Higher prevalence in South Asian, African American, Hispanic/Latino, Native American, and Pacific Islander populations.
  • History of gestational diabetes: People who had GDM have a 50% lifetime risk of developing T2D.
  • Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS): Associated with insulin resistance and increased T2D risk.

Modifiable Risk Factors

  • Overweight and obesity: Particularly central (abdominal) obesity. A BMI above 25 kg/m² increases risk; above 30 kg/m² increases it substantially.
  • Physical inactivity: Sedentary behavior reduces insulin sensitivity.
  • Unhealthy diet: High intake of processed foods, refined carbohydrates, sugary beverages, and low fiber intake.
  • Smoking: Active smokers have a 30–40% higher risk of developing T2D compared to non-smokers.
  • Sleep disorders: Obstructive sleep apnea and chronic sleep deprivation are linked to insulin resistance.
  • Stress: Chronic stress elevates cortisol, which raises blood glucose and promotes insulin resistance.

Tip: A single abnormal test result should be confirmed by repeating the same test or performing a different test on a subsequent day, unless the patient presents with unequivocal hyperglycemia or a hyperglycemic crisis.

Early screening is recommended for all adults aged 35 and older, and for younger adults who are overweight and have one or more additional risk factors. Screening should be repeated at least every three years if results are normal.

🌍 Global Burden and Economic Impact

Diabetes places an enormous burden on individuals, healthcare systems, and economies:

  • Approximately 6.7 million deaths were attributed to diabetes in 2021.
  • Global healthcare expenditure on diabetes was estimated at USD 966 billion in 2021.
  • Nearly 1 in 2 adults with diabetes remain undiagnosed worldwide (240 million people).
  • Diabetes increases the risk of hospitalization by 2–3 times compared to people without diabetes.
  • People with diabetes have 2–4 times higher cardiovascular mortality risk.
