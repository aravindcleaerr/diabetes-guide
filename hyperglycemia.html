Hyperglycemia (High Blood Sugar)

Understanding, managing, and preventing dangerous elevations in blood glucose

Content last reviewed: January 2026 | AI-assisted content
EMERGENCY WARNING: Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA) and Hyperosmolar Hyperglycemic State (HHS) are life-threatening emergencies. If you experience persistent vomiting, rapid breathing, confusion, fruity breath odor, or blood sugar above 300 mg/dL that won't come down — go to the nearest emergency room or call 112 (India) immediately.

⚠️ What Is Hyperglycemia?

Hyperglycemia is defined as blood glucose levels above the normal target range. For most people with diabetes, this means:

MeasurementTargetHyperglycemia
Fasting / pre-meal glucose80–130 mg/dL> 130 mg/dL
Post-meal glucose (1–2 hrs)< 180 mg/dL> 180 mg/dL
Random glucoseVaries> 200 mg/dL with symptoms

Occasional mild highs after meals are common and usually manageable. Persistent or severe hyperglycemia (> 250–300 mg/dL) requires prompt attention as it can progress to life-threatening emergencies.

🔍 Causes

  • Insufficient insulin or medication: Missed doses, inadequate dose, expired insulin, or injection into lipohypertrophic tissue (lumps from repeated injection in the same site) where absorption is impaired.
  • Excess carbohydrate intake: Eating more carbohydrate than the medication dose can cover.
  • Physical inactivity: Reduced activity decreases insulin sensitivity and glucose uptake by muscles.
  • Illness and infection: Stress hormones (cortisol, adrenaline, glucagon, growth hormone) released during illness raise blood glucose — even if the person is eating less.
  • Emotional stress: Psychological stress triggers the same counter-regulatory hormones.
  • Medications: Corticosteroids (prednisone, dexamethasone), certain antipsychotics, thiazide diuretics, and niacin can raise glucose levels.
  • Dawn phenomenon: A natural rise in blood glucose between 4:00 AM and 8:00 AM caused by overnight release of counter-regulatory hormones.
  • Pump malfunction: Infusion set kink, dislodged cannula, empty reservoir, or air bubbles in tubing can silently interrupt insulin delivery.
  • Rebound hyperglycemia (Somogyi effect): A counter-regulatory hormone surge following undetected nocturnal hypoglycemia.

🚨 Symptoms

  • Increased thirst (polydipsia)
  • Frequent urination (polyuria)
  • Blurred vision
  • Fatigue and weakness
  • Headache
  • Difficulty concentrating
  • Unexplained weight loss (when prolonged)
  • Slow wound healing
  • Recurrent infections (skin, urinary, vaginal)
Note: Mild hyperglycemia may have no noticeable symptoms at all. This is why regular blood glucose monitoring is essential — you cannot rely on symptoms alone.

🔥 Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)

🚨 EMERGENCY: DKA is fatal if untreated. Mortality 1–5% even with treatment. If glucose > 250 mg/dL with moderate/large ketones, seek emergency care immediately.

DKA is a serious, life-threatening emergency that occurs primarily in Type 1 diabetes but can also occur in Type 2 diabetes under severe stress (infection, surgery, myocardial infarction). It develops when the body has very little or no insulin, forcing it to break down fat for energy, producing ketone bodies (acetoacetate, beta-hydroxybutyrate, acetone) that acidify the blood.

Triggers

  • Missed or grossly inadequate insulin doses
  • New-onset Type 1 diabetes (DKA may be the initial presentation)
  • Insulin pump failure or infusion set disconnection
  • Acute illness or infection
  • Surgery, trauma, or severe emotional stress
  • Certain medications (SGLT2 inhibitors can cause euglycaemic DKA with near-normal glucose levels)

Symptoms of DKA

  • All symptoms of hyperglycemia (above), usually more intense
  • Nausea, vomiting, and abdominal pain
  • Fruity or acetone-smelling breath
  • Deep, rapid breathing (Kussmaul respiration) — the body's attempt to blow off excess acid as CO2
  • Severe dehydration (dry mouth, sunken eyes, reduced skin turgor)
  • Confusion, drowsiness, or altered consciousness
  • If untreated: coma and death

Treatment (Hospital-Based)

  • Aggressive IV fluid resuscitation: Typically normal saline (0.9% NaCl) to correct severe dehydration; average deficit is 5–7 litres in adults.
  • Continuous IV insulin infusion: Low-dose regular insulin to suppress ketogenesis and gradually lower glucose. Glucose should not be lowered faster than 50–75 mg/dL per hour.
  • Electrolyte replacement: Potassium is critical — insulin drives potassium into cells, and hypokalaemia can cause fatal cardiac arrhythmias. Potassium is replaced once levels are confirmed and urine output is adequate.
  • Monitoring: Hourly glucose, electrolytes every 2–4 hours, continuous cardiac monitoring, urine output.
  • Identify and treat the trigger: Infection, missed insulin, pump failure, etc.
Warning: DKA is fatal if untreated. Mortality is 1–5% even with treatment and rises sharply with delayed care, advanced age, or concurrent illness. If you have Type 1 diabetes and your glucose is above 250 mg/dL with moderate or large ketones, seek emergency medical care immediately.

💧 Hyperosmolar Hyperglycemic State (HHS)

🚨 EMERGENCY: HHS has 10–20% mortality — higher than DKA. Glucose often > 600 mg/dL with profound dehydration. Develops insidiously over days to weeks. Seek emergency care immediately.

HHS occurs predominantly in Type 2 diabetes, often in older adults. It is characterised by extreme hyperglycemia, severe dehydration, and hyperosmolarity without significant ketoacidosis.

Symptoms

  • Extreme thirst and dry mouth
  • Very frequent urination progressing to reduced urine output as dehydration worsens
  • Profound weakness and lethargy
  • Altered mental status (confusion, hallucinations, drowsiness, coma)
  • Seizures (in some cases)
  • Warm, dry skin without the Kussmaul breathing typically seen in DKA

Treatment

  • Vigorous IV fluid replacement is the cornerstone — fluid deficits are often even greater than in DKA.
  • IV insulin infusion (lower rates than DKA protocols in many cases).
  • Careful electrolyte monitoring and replacement.
  • Thromboprophylaxis — HHS carries a high risk of thromboembolic events due to hyperviscosity.
  • Identification and treatment of the precipitating cause (often infection, stroke, or medication non-adherence).
Warning: HHS has a higher mortality rate than DKA (10–20%). It often develops insidiously over days to weeks, especially in elderly patients with limited access to fluids or impaired thirst perception. Early recognition is critical.

🛑 When to Go to the Emergency Room

Seek emergency medical care immediately if you experience any of the following:

  • Blood glucose persistently above 300 mg/dL despite taking correction insulin doses
  • Moderate or large ketones on a urine or blood ketone test (blood ketones > 1.5 mmol/L)
  • Nausea and vomiting that prevent you from eating, drinking, or keeping down medication
  • Abdominal pain with high blood glucose
  • Fruity or acetone odour on the breath
  • Rapid, deep breathing (Kussmaul breathing)
  • Confusion, extreme drowsiness, or difficulty staying awake
  • Signs of severe dehydration (dizziness on standing, very dark urine, no urine output)
  • Fever above 101.3 F (38.5 C) combined with blood glucose above 250 mg/dL
Tip: If you use insulin, keep a blood ketone meter at home (e.g., Abbott Precision Xtra, Keto-Mojo). Blood ketone testing is faster and more reliable than urine ketone strips. Check ketones any time your glucose is above 250 mg/dL.

🤒 Sick Day Rules for High Blood Sugar

Illness causes blood glucose to rise even if you are eating less. Counter-regulatory stress hormones (cortisol, glucagon, adrenaline) increase glucose production and decrease insulin sensitivity. Follow these rules during any illness:

Essential Sick Day Guidelines

  • Never stop taking insulin. Even if you cannot eat, your body still needs basal insulin. Stopping insulin during illness is the single most common trigger for DKA.
  • Monitor glucose more frequently: Check every 2–4 hours (or use a CGM).
  • Check ketones: Test blood or urine ketones every 4–6 hours if glucose is above 250 mg/dL.
  • Stay hydrated: Drink at least 240 mL (8 oz) of water or sugar-free fluid every hour. If unable to eat, alternate between sugar-free and sugar-containing fluids to prevent both dehydration and hypoglycemia.
  • Use correction insulin: Follow your sick day insulin plan. Many protocols call for an additional 10–20% of total daily insulin dose as rapid-acting correction, or supplementary doses based on a correction factor.
  • Continue other diabetes medications unless specifically advised otherwise by your healthcare team. Exception: SGLT2 inhibitors should be paused during acute illness to reduce euglycaemic DKA risk.
  • Keep nourished: If unable to eat solid food, try soups, crackers, yoghurt, or regular (non-diet) gelatin. Aim for 45–50 g of carbohydrate every 3–4 hours.
  • Know when to call your diabetes team: If glucose remains above 250 mg/dL for more than two consecutive readings despite correction insulin, or if ketones are rising.
Note: Ask your endocrinologist or diabetes educator for a personalised sick day action plan BEFORE you get sick. Having a written plan on hand removes guesswork during illness.

🛡️ Prevention of Hyperglycemia

  • Take medications consistently: Set reminders or use a pill organiser. If using an insulin pump, check the infusion site daily for kinks, redness, or dislodgement.
  • Monitor blood glucose regularly: Use a CGM or test at the times agreed with your healthcare team (fasting, pre-meal, post-meal, bedtime).
  • Follow your meal plan: Be mindful of carbohydrate portions. Use carbohydrate counting or the plate method to maintain consistency.
  • Stay physically active: Aim for at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic activity per week, plus 2–3 sessions of resistance training.
  • Manage stress: Practice stress-reduction techniques (mindfulness, deep breathing, adequate sleep). Chronic stress elevates cortisol and raises glucose.
  • Store insulin correctly: Insulin degrades in heat (> 86 F / 30 C), direct sunlight, and freezing temperatures. Always check expiration dates.
  • Rotate injection and infusion sites: Prevent lipohypertrophy, which impairs insulin absorption and causes unpredictable glucose levels.
  • Regular follow-ups: See your endocrinologist every 3–6 months. Review HbA1c, CGM reports, time-in-range data, and adjust therapy as needed.
  • Have a sick day plan ready: Prepare in advance so you can act promptly when illness strikes.
Tip: Aim for a time-in-range (70–180 mg/dL) of at least 70%, which roughly corresponds to an HbA1c below 7%. Use CGM trend arrows and pattern analysis to proactively prevent highs rather than only reacting to them.
