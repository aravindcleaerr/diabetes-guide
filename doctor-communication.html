📋 Preparing for Appointments

Appointment Prep Checklist

  • Print or export glucose log (last 2-4 weeks)
  • List all current medications with doses
  • Gather recent lab reports
  • Write down questions for the doctor
  • Prepare food diary if discussing diet
  • Bring insurance card and medical ID
  • Invite a family member to join

A well-prepared appointment is a productive appointment. Doctors typically have limited time, so arriving organised ensures you cover everything that matters.

What to Bring

  • Glucose log: Paper logbook or app/CGM report (printed or on your phone) covering at least the past 2 weeks, ideally 4 weeks
  • Medication list: All current medications with doses and timing — including supplements, vitamins, and home remedies
  • Recent lab reports: Bring any tests done at outside labs
  • List of questions: Write down your questions beforehand — you will forget them in the moment
  • Food diary: If seeing a dietitian or discussing diet changes
  • Insurance card and medical ID
  • A family member or friend: A second pair of ears helps retain information
Keep a running "questions for the doctor" note on your phone. Add to it whenever something comes up between visits.

❓ What to Ask Your Doctor

Essential Questions for Every Visit

  • What is my current HbA1c, and what is my target?
  • Do any of my medications need to be changed or adjusted?
  • Am I due for any screening tests (eyes, kidneys, feet, lipids)?
  • Are there any new treatments or technologies I should consider?
  • What should my blood glucose targets be (fasting, pre-meal, post-meal, bedtime)?

Questions About Medications

  • What does this medication do, and why am I taking it?
  • What are the common side effects?
  • Should I take it with food or on an empty stomach?
  • What should I do if I miss a dose?
  • Can I switch to a more affordable alternative?

Questions About Complications

  • How are my kidneys, eyes, and feet doing based on recent screenings?
  • What symptoms should I watch for between appointments?
  • Am I at high risk for heart disease, and what can I do to reduce that risk?

Lifestyle Questions

  • What kind of exercise is safe for me?
  • Should I see a diabetes educator or dietitian?
  • Can you recommend a mental health professional experienced with diabetes?

📞 When to Call Between Appointments

Do not wait until your next scheduled visit if you experience:

  • Blood glucose consistently above 300 mg/dL despite medication
  • Severe or repeated hypoglycaemia (below 54 mg/dL)
  • Signs of DKA: nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, fruity breath
  • New or worsening symptoms: vision changes, foot wounds, chest pain, unexplained weight loss
  • Illness lasting more than 2 days with inability to keep food or fluids down
  • Medication side effects that interfere with daily life
  • Emotional distress, depression, or thoughts of self-harm
When in doubt, call. Your doctor would rather hear from you early than deal with a preventable emergency.
