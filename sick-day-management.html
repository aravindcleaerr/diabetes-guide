Sick Day Management

How to manage diabetes safely when you are unwell

Content last reviewed: January 2026 | AI-assisted content
IMPORTANT: Never stop taking insulin or diabetes medications during illness without your doctor's specific instruction. Illness can cause dangerous blood sugar swings. Contact your healthcare provider if you cannot keep food or fluids down, have blood sugar above 300 mg/dL, or detect ketones in your urine.
When to go to the ER: Persistent vomiting (6+ hrs), fruity breath or rapid deep breathing, blood ketones > 3.0 mmol/L, blood sugar > 500 mg/dL, confusion or altered consciousness, chest pain, or no urine for 8+ hours. Do not delay — call emergency services immediately.

📋 The Golden Rules

Never stop insulin without consulting your doctor. Even if you are not eating, your body still needs insulin. Illness increases counter-regulatory hormones (cortisol, glucagon, adrenaline), which raise blood sugar. Stopping insulin during illness is the most common cause of diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA).
Key principles when you are sick:
  • Continue taking your insulin or diabetes medications unless specifically told otherwise by your doctor
  • Monitor blood sugar every 2–4 hours
  • Check urine or blood ketones if blood sugar exceeds 240 mg/dL (especially type 1 diabetes)
  • Stay hydrated — sip fluids frequently
  • Have a sick-day kit ready at all times

📉 Blood Sugar Monitoring During Illness

  • Check blood glucose every 2–4 hours, even through the night if sugars are unstable
  • Record all readings with timestamps
  • If using a CGM (continuous glucose monitor), verify high readings with a finger-prick — CGM may lag during rapid changes
  • Blood sugar often runs higher during illness due to stress hormones, even if you are eating less
  • Occasionally, blood sugar may drop (e.g., gastroenteritis with vomiting and diarrhea) — be prepared for both scenarios

🧪 Checking Ketones

Blood ketone meters (measuring beta-hydroxybutyrate) are more accurate and timely than urine ketone strips. Keep a meter and strips in your sick-day kit.

💧 Staying Hydrated

Dehydration is dangerous during illness, especially with high blood sugars (osmotic diuresis) or vomiting/diarrhea.

Fluid Guidelines

  • Aim for at least 240 mL (1 cup) per hour while awake
  • If blood sugar is above 200 mg/dL: drink sugar-free fluids (water, clear broth, sugar-free electrolyte drinks, herbal tea)
  • If blood sugar is below 200 mg/dL or you cannot eat: alternate sugar-free fluids with small amounts of sugary fluids (regular soda, fruit juice, oral rehydration solution)
  • If vomiting, take small, frequent sips (1–2 tablespoons every 5 minutes) rather than large gulps
  • Oral rehydration solutions (ORS) are ideal — they replace both fluid and electrolytes

🍜 Sick Day Food Choices

If you cannot eat your regular meals, try to consume approximately 50 grams of carbohydrate every 3–4 hours in easy-to-digest forms:

  • Plain toast or crackers
  • Rice porridge (khichdi, congee)
  • Clear soups with noodles or rice
  • Yogurt or curd
  • Applesauce or mashed banana
  • Regular gelatin (Jello)
  • Ice pops (regular, not sugar-free, if blood sugar is low)
The goal is not perfect blood sugar control during illness — it is to prevent dangerous extremes (DKA or severe hypoglycemia) while maintaining nutrition and hydration.

📞 When to Call Your Doctor

Contact your diabetes care team if:

  • Blood sugar remains above 300 mg/dL for more than two consecutive readings despite corrective measures
  • Blood sugar drops below 70 mg/dL and does not respond to treatment
  • Ketones are moderate to high (blood ketones > 1.5 mmol/L)
  • You cannot keep fluids down for more than 4 hours
  • Fever persists beyond 24 hours or exceeds 101.5°F (38.6°C)
  • You are unsure about medication adjustments
  • Symptoms are worsening rather than improving

🚨 When to Go to the Emergency Room

Seek emergency care immediately if:
  • Persistent vomiting (cannot keep any fluids down for 6+ hours)
  • Signs of DKA: fruity breath, rapid deep breathing (Kussmaul), abdominal pain, confusion
  • Blood ketones > 3.0 mmol/L
  • Blood sugar > 500 mg/dL
  • Signs of severe dehydration: dizziness on standing, very dry mouth, no urine for 8+ hours, sunken eyes
  • Altered consciousness, confusion, or inability to stay awake
  • Chest pain or difficulty breathing

💊 Medication Adjustments During Illness

Insulin

  • Never stop basal (long-acting) insulin — you may need to increase it by 10–20%
  • Supplemental rapid-acting insulin (correction doses) may be needed every 4 hours
  • If blood sugars are running low (due to vomiting/poor intake), reduce bolus doses but maintain basal insulin
  • Pump users: consider a temporary basal rate increase of 20–50%

Oral Medications

The acronym "SADMANS" lists medications to hold during sick days: SGLT2 inhibitors, ACE inhibitors / ARBs, Diuretics, Metformin, NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen). Discuss this with your doctor in advance.

🤒 Managing Vomiting

  • Wait 15–30 minutes after vomiting, then try small sips of clear fluid
  • Anti-emetics (ondansetron, domperidone) — ask your doctor for a prescription to keep at home
  • Avoid solid food until vomiting has stopped for at least 2 hours
  • Start with bland, easy-to-digest foods (toast, crackers, plain rice)
  • If you took rapid-acting insulin before a meal and then vomited, monitor closely for hypoglycemia — you may need glucose tablets or juice

🧰 Sick Day Kit — Be Prepared

Keep the following assembled and accessible at all times:

  • Glucose meter, extra strips, and lancets
  • Blood or urine ketone testing supplies
  • Glucose tablets or gel
  • Oral rehydration solution (ORS) packets
  • Anti-emetic medication (prescribed by your doctor)
  • Thermometer
  • A written sick-day plan from your doctor with specific instructions and emergency contacts
  • List of all current medications with doses
Discuss your sick-day plan with your doctor BEFORE you get sick. Having a plan in advance prevents panic and delayed action during illness.
