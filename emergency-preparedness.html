Emergency Preparedness

Be ready for diabetes emergencies, natural disasters, and unexpected situations

Diabetes emergencies can be life-threatening. Always have your emergency kit ready, know the warning signs, and ensure family members know what to do. Call 112 (India) or go to the nearest ER if in doubt.

🛡️ Diabetes Emergency Kit

Every person with diabetes should have a ready-to-go emergency kit. Keep it in an accessible location and check it monthly for expired items.

🌊 Natural Disaster Preparation

Floods, earthquakes, cyclones, and other disasters can disrupt access to medications, electricity, and medical care for days or weeks.

  • Keep a 30-day backup supply of all medications and insulin at all times
  • Store your emergency kit in a waterproof bag
  • Have a list of pharmacies and hospitals along your evacuation route
  • Know your nearest emergency shelter and whether it can accommodate medical needs
  • Register with local disaster preparedness programs if available
  • Keep a photocopy or digital scan of all prescriptions on your phone and in cloud storage

⚡ Power Outage & Insulin Storage

Insulin must be stored properly to remain effective:

  • Unopened insulin: Refrigerate at 2–8 degrees C. Without power, it can survive at room temperature (below 30 degrees C) for up to 28 days (varies by type — check the package insert)
  • In-use insulin: Can be kept at room temperature (below 30 degrees C) for 28 days (most types)
  • Never freeze insulin — frozen insulin is permanently damaged and must be discarded
  • Never expose to direct sunlight or heat above 30 degrees C
  • Cooling solutions: FRIO cooling wallets (evaporative cooling — no electricity needed), insulated lunch bags with ice packs, clay pot / matka cooling
FRIO wallets are inexpensive, reusable, and work for 45+ hours — highly recommended for Indian summers, travel, and emergencies. Available on Amazon India for Rs 500–1500.

💊 Medication Supply — The 30-Day Rule

  • Always refill prescriptions when you have at least 7 days of supply remaining
  • Aim to maintain a rolling 30-day backup of all diabetes medications
  • Ask your doctor for a written prescription that you can keep in your emergency kit
  • If travelling, carry medications in your hand luggage — never check them in
  • Store a copy of your prescription on your phone (photo or PDF)
  • Know which of your medications are available over-the-counter at pharmacies in an emergency

📇 Emergency Contacts Card

Create a card (wallet-sized) and also save in your phone's emergency/medical ID feature:

  • Name and diabetes type (Type 1 / Type 2 / other)
  • Current medications and doses
  • Allergies
  • Endocrinologist name and phone number
  • Emergency contact (family member name and phone)
  • Nearest hospital with emergency department
  • Insurance / health card number
On Android: Settings > Safety & Emergency > Medical Information. On iPhone: Health app > Medical ID. Paramedics are trained to check these.

🏥 Hospital Bag Checklist

If you need to go to the hospital (planned or emergency), bring:

  • All current medications in original packaging
  • Glucose meter, strips, and lancets
  • Insulin and injection supplies
  • CGM receiver/reader if applicable
  • Insulin pump supplies (if applicable): extra infusion sets, reservoirs, batteries
  • Glucose tablets and snacks
  • List of medications and allergies (printed)
  • Insurance card and ID proof
  • Doctor's contact details
  • Phone charger
  • Comfortable, loose-fitting clothes and footwear (for diabetic feet)
