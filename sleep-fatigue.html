Sleep & Fatigue

How diabetes affects sleep and what you can do about it

Content last reviewed: January 2026 | AI-assisted content

🔄 The Sleep-Diabetes Connection

Sleep and blood sugar have a powerful bidirectional relationship. Poor sleep worsens insulin resistance and glycemic control, while uncontrolled diabetes disrupts sleep through multiple mechanisms.

  • Sleeping less than 6 hours or more than 9 hours is associated with higher HbA1c
  • Even one night of sleep deprivation can reduce insulin sensitivity by 25%
  • Poor sleep increases cortisol and growth hormone, both of which raise blood sugar
  • Sleep fragmentation increases appetite (via ghrelin) and reduces satiety (via leptin), promoting weight gain

🛌 Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA)

OSA is extremely common in type 2 diabetes — prevalence ranges from 58% to 86% depending on the study and diagnostic criteria. Most cases are undiagnosed.

What Happens

During sleep, the upper airway collapses repeatedly, causing pauses in breathing (apneas) lasting 10 seconds or more. Each episode triggers a micro-arousal, oxygen desaturation, and a surge of stress hormones.

How OSA Worsens Diabetes

  • Intermittent hypoxia increases insulin resistance
  • Sympathetic nervous system activation raises blood sugar
  • Sleep fragmentation disrupts glucose-regulating hormones
  • Daytime sleepiness reduces physical activity and worsens dietary choices

Symptoms Suggesting OSA

  • Loud snoring (often reported by a partner)
  • Witnessed breathing pauses during sleep
  • Gasping or choking during sleep
  • Excessive daytime sleepiness despite adequate sleep duration
  • Morning headaches
  • Difficulty concentrating
  • Nocturia (frequent nighttime urination)

Screening

The STOP-BANG questionnaire is a simple screening tool. A score of 3 or more suggests high risk.

All people with type 2 diabetes, especially those who are overweight or obese, should be screened for OSA. Ask your doctor about a home sleep study.

CPAP Benefits for Glucose Control

Continuous Positive Airway Pressure (CPAP) is the gold-standard treatment for moderate-to-severe OSA:

  • Reduces insulin resistance within days of starting treatment
  • May lower HbA1c by 0.4–0.7% with consistent use (more than 4 hours/night)
  • Reduces blood pressure (often by 3–5 mmHg)
  • Improves daytime alertness and energy
  • Reduces cardiovascular risk
CPAP must be used consistently (ideally every night, for the entire sleep period) to see glycemic benefits. Adherence is the biggest challenge — work with a sleep specialist to optimize mask fit and comfort.

🌙 Sleep Hygiene Tips for People with Diabetes

⏰Consistent ScheduleSame bedtime and wake time every day, including weekends
🌑Dark & Cool RoomBlackout curtains, 65-68 F, white noise if needed
📵Screen-Free Wind-DownNo screens 30 min before bed; try reading or stretching
📉Bedtime BG 100-180Avoid sleeping with very high or low blood sugar levels
  • Consistent schedule — go to bed and wake up at the same time every day, including weekends
  • Dark, cool, quiet room — use blackout curtains, earplugs, or a white-noise machine
  • Limit screens — avoid phones, tablets, and computers for at least 30 minutes before bed
  • Avoid caffeine after 2 PM
  • Limit alcohol — it disrupts sleep architecture and can cause nocturnal hypoglycemia
  • Evening blood sugar — avoid going to bed with very high or very low blood sugar; target 100–180 mg/dL at bedtime
  • Light evening snack — if prone to nocturnal hypoglycemia, a small snack with protein and complex carbs (e.g., a few crackers with peanut butter) before bed can help
  • Regular exercise — but complete vigorous exercise at least 3 hours before bedtime
  • Relaxation routine — warm bath, gentle stretching, reading, or deep breathing before bed

🦶 Restless Legs Syndrome (RLS)

RLS is more common in people with diabetes, likely due to peripheral neuropathy and iron deficiency.

  • Uncomfortable sensations (crawling, tingling, aching) in the legs, worse at rest and in the evening
  • An irresistible urge to move the legs
  • Symptoms improve temporarily with movement
  • Severely disrupts falling asleep and staying asleep

Management

  • Check and correct iron and ferritin levels (target ferritin > 75 ng/mL)
  • Optimize blood sugar control (neuropathy is a contributor)
  • Gabapentin or pregabalin (also used for neuropathic pain — dual benefit)
  • Dopamine agonists (pramipexole, ropinirole) for severe cases
  • Avoid triggers: caffeine, alcohol, antihistamines, certain antidepressants

🚽 Nocturia

Frequent nighttime urination is a common cause of sleep disruption in diabetes:

  • Osmotic diuresis — when blood sugar is high, excess glucose spills into urine, pulling water with it
  • Diabetic bladder dysfunction — autonomic neuropathy causes incomplete emptying and increased frequency
  • SGLT2 inhibitors — these medications intentionally increase glucose excretion in urine and can worsen nocturia
  • Concurrent conditions — heart failure, enlarged prostate, urinary tract infections

Management

  • Optimize blood sugar control — this is the most effective measure
  • Limit fluid intake 2–3 hours before bed
  • Take SGLT2 inhibitors in the morning
  • Elevate legs in the evening (reduces peripheral edema and nocturnal fluid shifts)
  • Evaluate for other causes (prostate, UTI, heart failure)

💤 Managing Fatigue

Fatigue is one of the most common complaints among people with diabetes. Causes include:

  • Blood sugar fluctuations (both highs and lows cause fatigue)
  • Sleep disorders (OSA, RLS, nocturia)
  • Depression and diabetes distress
  • Anemia (common in diabetic kidney disease)
  • Hypothyroidism (autoimmune thyroid disease is common in type 1 diabetes)
  • Medications (beta-blockers, antihistamines, certain antidepressants)
  • Deconditioning from physical inactivity

Strategies to Combat Fatigue

  • Stabilize blood sugars — reduce glycemic variability with diet, medication timing, and activity
  • Regular physical activity — even a 10-minute walk after meals boosts energy
  • Stay hydrated — dehydration worsens fatigue; aim for 2–3 liters of water daily
  • Screen for and treat OSA, depression, thyroid disease, and anemia
  • Balanced meals — avoid large carbohydrate-heavy meals that cause post-meal crashes
  • Short naps (15–20 minutes) if needed, but avoid long or late afternoon naps
If fatigue is persistent and unexplained, ask your doctor to check: HbA1c, complete blood count, thyroid function, vitamin B12, vitamin D, and kidney function.

🩺 When to Get a Sleep Study

Discuss a sleep study (polysomnography or home sleep test) with your doctor if you have:

  • Loud snoring with witnessed apneas
  • Excessive daytime sleepiness despite 7+ hours in bed
  • BMI > 30 with unexplained fatigue
  • Resistant hypertension (blood pressure hard to control despite 3 medications)
  • Refractory high blood sugars despite good dietary and medication adherence
  • Morning headaches or dry mouth
