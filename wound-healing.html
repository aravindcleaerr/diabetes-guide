Wound Healing & Surgery Care

Why diabetes impairs healing and how to optimize outcomes before and after surgery

🔬 Why Diabetes Impairs Wound Healing

Normal wound healing is a complex, well-orchestrated process involving inflammation, cell proliferation, and tissue remodeling. Diabetes disrupts every phase:

Poor Circulation
Diabetes damages both large arteries (peripheral arterial disease) and small blood vessels (microangiopathy). Reduced blood flow means less oxygen, fewer nutrients, and fewer immune cells reaching the wound site. Tissue at the wound edges may be ischemic even when blood flow elsewhere seems adequate.
Neuropathy
Peripheral neuropathy means you may not feel injuries when they occur. A small cut, blister, or foreign object in a shoe can go unnoticed for days, allowing infection to establish and tissue damage to progress. This is the primary reason diabetic foot ulcers are so common and dangerous.
Immune Dysfunction
Hyperglycemia impairs white blood cell function — neutrophils migrate more slowly, phagocytosis (engulfing bacteria) is reduced, and the oxidative burst (bacterial killing mechanism) is blunted. Blood glucose above 200 mg/dL significantly reduces immune cell effectiveness, essentially creating a state of relative immunosuppression.
High Glucose Feeds Bacteria
Glucose-rich wound fluid is an ideal growth medium for bacteria. Common wound pathogens — Staphylococcus aureus, Pseudomonas, E. coli, and anaerobes — proliferate rapidly in hyperglycemic environments. Biofilm formation (antibiotic-resistant bacterial colonies) is enhanced in high-glucose conditions.
Impaired Collagen Synthesis
Wound strength depends on new collagen deposition. Hyperglycemia reduces fibroblast proliferation and collagen production. AGEs (advanced glycation end products) cross-link existing collagen fibers, making them stiff and dysfunctional. The result: wounds are weaker and more likely to reopen or break down.
Prolonged Inflammation
In diabetes, the inflammatory phase of wound healing is prolonged and dysregulated. Instead of a brief, controlled inflammatory response that transitions to the rebuilding phase, the wound remains stuck in a chronic inflammatory state, with excessive matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) breaking down new tissue as fast as it forms.

🎯 Wound Care Basics

Proper wound care can dramatically improve healing outcomes even in diabetes. Follow these fundamental principles:

Clean the Wound
Gently wash with clean running water or normal saline. Remove any debris. Do NOT use hydrogen peroxide, iodine, or alcohol directly on open wounds — they are cytotoxic and kill the healthy cells needed for healing. Mild soap around (not in) the wound is acceptable.
Keep It Moist
Contrary to old beliefs, wounds heal faster in a moist environment — not "dried out." Use appropriate dressings: hydrogels, foam dressings, or hydrocolloids maintain optimal moisture. A moist wound bed promotes cell migration, reduces pain, and prevents scab formation that impedes healing.
Protect from Infection
Cover the wound with a sterile dressing. Change dressings daily or when soiled. Apply a thin layer of antibiotic ointment (mupirocin, silver sulfadiazine) if recommended by your doctor. Keep the wound covered — exposure to air increases contamination risk.
Control Blood Sugar
Target blood glucose 100–180 mg/dL during active wound healing. Tighter control (100–140 mg/dL) is ideal if achievable without hypoglycemia. Every 50 mg/dL increase above 150 slows healing measurably. You may need temporary insulin even if you normally take oral medications.
Offload Pressure
For foot wounds, pressure offloading is critical. Total contact casting, removable cast walkers, or therapeutic footwear redistribute weight away from the ulcer. Walking on an open foot wound — even with bandaging — prevents healing and risks catastrophic infection.
Ensure Nutrition
Wound healing demands extra protein (1.2–1.5 g/kg/day), vitamin C (500–1000 mg/day), zinc (15–30 mg/day), and adequate calories. Malnutrition is a common and correctable cause of poor healing. Ensure adequate protein intake — eggs, paneer, dal, lean meat, soy.

🚨 Signs of Wound Infection

Seek Immediate Medical Attention If You Notice Any of These

  • Increasing redness spreading outward from the wound edges (cellulitis). A red streak traveling up a limb toward the body is especially urgent — it may indicate spreading infection (lymphangitis).
  • Warmth and swelling around the wound that is increasing rather than decreasing over days.
  • Pus or foul-smelling discharge — yellow, green, or brown drainage with an offensive odor indicates bacterial infection. Clear to light yellow serous drainage is normal; thick, opaque, or malodorous drainage is not.
  • Fever above 100.4°F (38°C) — systemic infection. Combined with a wound, this requires urgent medical evaluation and likely IV antibiotics.
  • Increasing pain at the wound site. In people with diabetes and neuropathy, the absence of pain does NOT mean the wound is fine — always inspect visually.
  • Wound becoming larger or deeper despite care — this suggests underlying infection, poor blood supply, or both.
  • Black or grey tissue at the wound base — this is necrotic (dead) tissue that must be debrided by a healthcare professional.
  • Exposed bone, tendon, or joint — this is a deep wound requiring immediate surgical consultation and likely hospitalization.
  • Crepitus (crackling feeling under skin) — suggests gas-forming bacterial infection (gas gangrene), a surgical emergency.

🏥 Pre-Surgery Preparation for Diabetic Patients

If you have diabetes and need surgery (elective or semi-urgent), careful preparation significantly reduces complications:

4–8 Weeks Before Surgery — Optimize HbA1c
Target HbA1c below 8% (ideally below 7.5%) before elective surgery. If HbA1c is above 9%, consider delaying non-urgent surgery until control improves. Work with your endocrinologist to intensify treatment — adding insulin, adjusting medications, or tightening diet. Poorly controlled diabetes increases surgical site infection risk by 3–5 times.
1–2 Weeks Before — Pre-Operative Assessment
Get comprehensive labs: HbA1c, fasting glucose, kidney function (creatinine, eGFR), electrolytes, CBC, coagulation profile. ECG and echocardiogram if cardiac risk factors exist. Discuss your medication plan with both surgeon and anesthesiologist. Stop metformin 24–48 hours before surgery (risk of lactic acidosis with contrast dye or reduced kidney perfusion). SGLT2 inhibitors should be stopped 3 days before surgery (risk of euglycemic diabetic ketoacidosis).
Night Before / Morning Of Surgery
Follow fasting instructions precisely. If on basal insulin: take 75–80% of your usual dose the night before. Do NOT take morning rapid-acting insulin or oral diabetes medications on the day of surgery. Check blood glucose on arrival — target 100–180 mg/dL. Inform the surgical team of your last insulin dose and current glucose level. Bring your glucometer and medications to the hospital.
During Surgery — Glucose Management
Target intraoperative blood glucose: 140–180 mg/dL. This range balances infection prevention (glucose <180) with hypoglycemia avoidance (glucose >140). Insulin infusions are used during major surgeries to maintain this range. Tighter control (<140) does not improve outcomes and increases hypoglycemia risk. The anesthesiology team manages glucose during the procedure.
Post-Surgery — Recovery Phase
Glucose monitoring every 4–6 hours (or more frequently with insulin drip). Transition from IV to subcutaneous insulin when eating resumes. Resume oral medications once eating normally and kidney function is stable. Restart metformin after 48 hours if eGFR is adequate. Watch for surgical site infection closely for 30 days. Ensure adequate protein intake for wound healing. Early mobilization reduces DVT risk and improves glucose control.

🧰 Advanced Wound Therapies

For chronic wounds (those not healing after 4–6 weeks of standard care), advanced therapies may be indicated:

  • Negative Pressure Wound Therapy (NPWT / VAC therapy): A sealed dressing connected to a vacuum pump that applies controlled negative pressure to the wound bed. This removes excess fluid, reduces edema, increases blood flow, and promotes granulation tissue formation. Highly effective for large, deep, or complex wounds. Changed every 48–72 hours.
  • Hyperbaric Oxygen Therapy (HBOT): You breathe 100% oxygen in a pressurized chamber (2–2.5 atmospheres) for 60–90 minutes per session, typically 20–40 sessions. This dramatically increases tissue oxygen levels, enhances white blood cell killing of bacteria, stimulates new blood vessel growth (angiogenesis), and promotes collagen synthesis. Best evidence for diabetic foot ulcers with underlying ischemia. Expensive and time-consuming but effective when other therapies fail.
  • Bioengineered Skin Substitutes: Artificial skin products (Apligraf, Dermagraft) provide growth factors and a scaffold for new tissue formation. Applied to clean, debrided wound beds. Used when wounds fail to respond to 4+ weeks of standard care.
  • Growth Factor Therapy: Topical platelet-derived growth factor (becaplermin/Regranex) stimulates cell proliferation and wound closure. Applied daily to clean ulcers. Modest benefit demonstrated in clinical trials.
  • Surgical Debridement: Regular removal of dead, damaged, or infected tissue is essential for chronic wounds. Sharp debridement (scalpel or curette) is the gold standard. Enzymatic debridement (collagenase ointment) is a non-surgical alternative. Autolytic debridement (moisture-retentive dressings) is the gentlest approach.

🚨 When to See a Doctor

  • Any wound that has not shown improvement after 2 weeks of proper home care.
  • Any foot wound if you have diabetes — all diabetic foot wounds should be evaluated by a healthcare professional, even if they appear minor.
  • Wounds near joints, on the face, or in the genital area.
  • Deep wounds where fat, muscle, tendon, or bone may be visible.
  • Animal or human bites.
  • Puncture wounds (especially through footwear into the sole of the foot).
  • Any wound with signs of infection (redness, warmth, pus, fever).
  • Wounds in people on immunosuppressive medications or with kidney disease.
  • Any wound accompanied by numbness — neuropathy means you cannot accurately assess wound severity by feel alone.
