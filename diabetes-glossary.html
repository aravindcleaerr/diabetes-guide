Diabetes Glossary

Key terms and definitions you will encounter on your diabetes journey

Content last reviewed: January 2026 | AI-assisted content

🔠 A–C

TermDefinition
AutoimmuneA condition in which the body's immune system mistakenly attacks its own cells. In Type 1 diabetes, the immune system destroys insulin-producing beta cells.
Basal InsulinLong-acting or intermediate-acting insulin that provides a steady, low-level background supply of insulin throughout the day and night. Examples: Lantus (glargine), Tresiba (degludec), Levemir (detemir), NPH.
Beta CellsCells located in the islets of Langerhans in the pancreas that produce, store, and release insulin. Destruction of beta cells causes Type 1 diabetes; dysfunction causes Type 2.
Blood GlucoseThe concentration of glucose (sugar) in the blood, measured in mg/dL (India, US) or mmol/L (UK). Normal fasting: 70–100 mg/dL.
Bolus InsulinRapid-acting or short-acting insulin taken before meals to cover the rise in blood glucose from food. Examples: NovoRapid (aspart), Humalog (lispro), Apidra (glulisine), regular insulin.
CGM (Continuous Glucose Monitor)A wearable device with a tiny sensor inserted under the skin that measures glucose levels in interstitial fluid every 1–5 minutes and displays trends. Examples: FreeStyle Libre, Dexcom G6/G7, Medtronic Guardian.
C-PeptideA protein released alongside insulin by the pancreas. Measuring C-peptide helps determine how much insulin the body is still producing. Low C-peptide suggests Type 1 or late-stage Type 2.
Carb CountingA meal-planning method where you count the grams of carbohydrate in each meal to match insulin doses accurately.

🔤 D–G

TermDefinition
DKA (Diabetic Ketoacidosis)A life-threatening emergency caused by severe insulin deficiency, leading to very high blood sugar, ketone production, and blood acidification. Most common in Type 1 but can occur in Type 2. Symptoms: nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, fruity breath, rapid breathing. Requires immediate hospitalisation.
Dawn PhenomenonA natural rise in blood glucose in the early morning hours (4–8 AM) caused by hormonal changes (cortisol, growth hormone). Common in both Type 1 and Type 2.
eGFR (Estimated Glomerular Filtration Rate)A blood test measuring kidney function. Normal: > 90 mL/min. Below 60 indicates chronic kidney disease. Calculated from serum creatinine, age, sex, and race.
Fasting GlucoseBlood glucose level measured after at least 8 hours of not eating. Normal: 70–100 mg/dL. Prediabetes: 100–125 mg/dL. Diabetes: 126 mg/dL or higher.
GAD Antibodies (Glutamic Acid Decarboxylase)Antibodies that attack beta cells. A positive GAD antibody test helps confirm autoimmune diabetes (Type 1 or LADA).
GLP-1 (Glucagon-Like Peptide 1)A gut hormone that stimulates insulin secretion, suppresses glucagon, slows gastric emptying, and promotes satiety. GLP-1 receptor agonist medications (e.g., liraglutide/Victoza, semaglutide/Ozempic) mimic this hormone.
Glycaemic Index (GI)A scale from 0–100 ranking foods by how quickly they raise blood glucose. Low GI (< 55): lentils, most vegetables, whole grains. High GI (> 70): white rice, white bread, potatoes.
GlucagonA hormone produced by alpha cells in the pancreas that raises blood glucose by stimulating the liver to release stored glucose. Used as an emergency injection/nasal spray for severe hypoglycaemia.

🔡 H–K

TermDefinition
HbA1c (Glycated Haemoglobin)A blood test showing average blood glucose over the past 2–3 months. Expressed as a percentage. Normal: < 5.7%. Prediabetes: 5.7–6.4%. Diabetes: ≥ 6.5%. Target for most diabetics: < 7%.
HHS (Hyperosmolar Hyperglycaemic State)A life-threatening emergency in Type 2 diabetes characterised by extremely high blood sugar (> 600 mg/dL), severe dehydration, and altered consciousness, without significant ketosis. Requires immediate hospitalisation.
HyperglycaemiaHigh blood glucose, generally defined as above 180 mg/dL (post-meal) or above 130 mg/dL (fasting). Chronic hyperglycaemia causes long-term diabetes complications.
HypoglycaemiaLow blood glucose, generally below 70 mg/dL. Symptoms: shakiness, sweating, confusion, rapid heartbeat. Severe hypoglycaemia (below 54 mg/dL) can cause seizures or loss of consciousness. Treated with fast-acting glucose.
InsulinA hormone produced by beta cells in the pancreas that allows glucose to enter cells for energy. People with Type 1 produce no insulin; people with Type 2 produce insufficient insulin or cannot use it effectively.
Insulin ResistanceA condition in which cells do not respond normally to insulin, requiring the pancreas to produce more to achieve the same effect. Central to the pathophysiology of Type 2 diabetes, prediabetes, and metabolic syndrome.
Islets of LangerhansClusters of cells in the pancreas containing alpha cells (glucagon), beta cells (insulin), delta cells (somatostatin), and other endocrine cells.
KetonesChemicals produced when the body breaks down fat for energy instead of glucose. Small amounts are normal during fasting; high levels (ketonuria/ketonaemia) in a diabetic indicate insulin deficiency and can lead to DKA.

🔢 L–O

TermDefinition
LADA (Latent Autoimmune Diabetes in Adults)A slowly progressing form of autoimmune diabetes in adults (often misdiagnosed as Type 2). Positive for autoantibodies (GAD), but may not need insulin immediately. Sometimes called "Type 1.5."
LipodystrophyAbnormal fat tissue at injection sites — either lumps (lipohypertrophy) or indentations (lipoatrophy) — caused by repeated injections in the same area. Affects insulin absorption. Prevented by proper site rotation.
Macrovascular ComplicationsDiabetes complications affecting large blood vessels: coronary artery disease (heart attack), cerebrovascular disease (stroke), and peripheral arterial disease.
Microvascular ComplicationsDiabetes complications affecting small blood vessels: retinopathy (eyes), nephropathy (kidneys), and neuropathy (nerves).
Nephropathy (Diabetic)Kidney damage caused by diabetes. Detected by elevated UACR (albumin in urine) and reduced eGFR. Can progress to end-stage kidney disease requiring dialysis or transplant if uncontrolled.
Neuropathy (Diabetic)Nerve damage caused by diabetes. Most common is peripheral neuropathy (tingling, numbness, pain in feet and hands). Autonomic neuropathy affects internal organs (heart, digestion, bladder).
OGTT (Oral Glucose Tolerance Test)A diagnostic test: blood glucose is measured fasting and 2 hours after drinking 75 g of glucose. Used to diagnose diabetes and gestational diabetes. 2-hour value ≥ 200 mg/dL = diabetes.

🔣 P–S

TermDefinition
PancreasAn organ behind the stomach that produces insulin and glucagon (endocrine function) and digestive enzymes (exocrine function).
Peripheral Arterial Disease (PAD)Narrowing of arteries in the legs and feet due to atherosclerosis. Common in diabetics. Symptoms: leg pain on walking (claudication), poor wound healing, cold feet. Risk of amputation if severe.
PostprandialAfter a meal. Postprandial blood glucose is typically measured 2 hours after starting a meal. Target: < 180 mg/dL for most diabetics.
Retinopathy (Diabetic)Damage to blood vessels in the retina caused by chronic hyperglycaemia. Leading cause of blindness in working-age adults. Stages: mild, moderate, severe non-proliferative; proliferative. Detected by dilated eye exam.
SGLT2 InhibitorsA class of oral diabetes medications that work by blocking glucose reabsorption in the kidneys, causing excess glucose to be excreted in urine. Examples: dapagliflozin (Forxiga), empagliflozin (Jardiance), canagliflozin (Invokana). Also provide heart and kidney protection.
SulfonylureaA class of oral diabetes medications that stimulate the pancreas to produce more insulin. Examples: glimepiride, gliclazide, glipizide. Risk of hypoglycaemia and weight gain.

🔚 T–Z

TermDefinition
Time in Range (TIR)The percentage of time blood glucose stays within the target range (typically 70–180 mg/dL) over a given period. Measured by CGM. Target: > 70%. Higher TIR correlates with lower complication risk.
Type 1 DiabetesAn autoimmune disease in which the immune system destroys beta cells, resulting in absolute insulin deficiency. Requires lifelong insulin therapy. Typically diagnosed in children and young adults but can occur at any age (including as LADA).
Type 2 DiabetesA metabolic disease characterised by insulin resistance and progressive beta cell dysfunction. Accounts for ~90% of all diabetes cases. Managed with lifestyle changes, oral medications, and/or insulin. Strong genetic and lifestyle risk factors.
UACR (Urine Albumin-to-Creatinine Ratio)A urine test for early kidney damage. Normal: < 30 mg/g. Moderately increased albuminuria: 30–300 mg/g. Severely increased: > 300 mg/g.
