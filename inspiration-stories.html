🌟 Famous People Living with Diabetes

Halle Berry — Type 1 (initially diagnosed Type 2, later reclassified)

Academy Award-winning actress Halle Berry was diagnosed with diabetes in their 20s after collapsing on a TV set and falling into a diabetic coma. Berry has spoken openly about the journey, emphasising the importance of diet, exercise, and never letting diabetes define you. Berry has maintained a successful Hollywood career spanning decades while managing the condition daily.

Tom Hanks — Type 2

Two-time Oscar winner Tom Hanks was diagnosed with Type 2 diabetes in 2013 after years of elevated blood sugar. Hanks has been candid about the role of weight gain and lifestyle in the diagnosis, and has used a public platform to raise awareness about the condition. Hanks manages diabetes with medication, diet, and exercise.

Nick Jonas — Type 1

Singer and actor Nick Jonas was diagnosed with Type 1 diabetes at age 13. Jonas noticed symptoms -- excessive thirst, weight loss, and mood changes -- and was diagnosed quickly. Jonas wears an OmniPod insulin pump and a Dexcom CGM, and has been a vocal advocate for diabetes awareness through the nonprofit Beyond Type 1. Jonas continues to perform worldwide, tour, and live a full life.

Wasim Akram — Type 1

Legendary Pakistani cricket fast bowler Wasim Akram was diagnosed with Type 1 diabetes during an active cricket career. Akram continued playing international cricket at the highest level while managing diabetes with insulin. Akram is now a prominent diabetes awareness advocate in South Asia.

Sonia Sotomayor — Type 1

United States Supreme Court Justice Sonia Sotomayor was diagnosed with Type 1 diabetes at age 7. Sotomayor learned to self-administer insulin injections as a child and has managed the condition for over five decades while rising to the highest court in the country. The memoir describes how diabetes taught discipline and resilience.

Kamal Haasan — Type 2

Iconic Indian actor Kamal Haasan has spoken publicly about managing Type 2 diabetes. Despite a demanding career in film and politics, Haasan manages the condition through medication and lifestyle modifications.

💪 Real Patient Success Stories

A Journey to Type 2 Remission

One individual was diagnosed with Type 2 diabetes in their late 30s with an HbA1c of 9.2%. The doctor started them on metformin and glimepiride, but they also committed to walking 45 minutes daily, switching to millet-based rotis, reducing rice portions by half, and eliminating sugary chai. Within 18 months, their HbA1c dropped to 5.8%, and the doctor was able to stop glimepiride entirely. They continue on metformin and maintain their lifestyle changes. "The diagnosis was a wake-up call. I am healthier now than I was years ago."

Living Well with LADA

Another individual was diagnosed with LADA (Latent Autoimmune Diabetes in Adults) at age 40, initially treated as Type 2 before antibody tests confirmed autoimmune diabetes. After switching to insulin therapy and starting a CGM, their Time in Range improved from 45% to 78%. They run a small business, play badminton three times a week, and mentor newly diagnosed adults in an online support group. "Insulin gave me my life back. I wish I had started it sooner."

Type 1 Since Childhood

Diagnosed at age 8, one person grew up managing Type 1 diabetes through school, college, and now a career in graphic design. They use an insulin pump and CGM, and track carbs meticulously. They completed a half-marathon in 2024 and blog about living with Type 1 in India. "Diabetes is part of my story, not the whole story."

Reversing Complications

One person was diagnosed with Type 2 diabetes at 45 and largely ignored it for a decade. By age 55, they had early nephropathy (UACR 180 mg/g) and mild retinopathy. A stern conversation with a new endocrinologist was the turning point. They started insulin, an SGLT2 inhibitor, controlled blood pressure aggressively, and lost 12 kg. Five years later, their UACR improved to 45 mg/g and retinopathy stabilised. "I regret the years I wasted, but I am grateful it is not too late."

💡 Key Takeaways

  • Consistency beats perfection: You do not need perfect blood sugars every day. Consistent good habits over time yield remarkable results
  • Support matters: Whether from family, friends, online communities, or a great care team — no one should manage diabetes alone
  • A positive attitude is therapeutic: Studies show that diabetes distress and depression worsen outcomes. Seeking joy, maintaining hobbies, and staying socially connected are part of treatment
  • Technology is an enabler: CGMs, insulin pumps, apps, and telemedicine have made diabetes management easier than ever before
  • It is never too late: Whether you are newly diagnosed or have been living with diabetes for decades, improving your management today will benefit your future self

🤝 Community Stories

Connecting with others who share your experience is powerful. Here are ways to find and share community stories:

  • Beyond Type 1: Online community platform with thousands of personal stories from people with Type 1 diabetes worldwide
  • Diabetes India Community: Facebook groups where Indian patients share experiences, tips, and support
  • DiabetesForums.com: One of the oldest online forums with active community discussions
  • Reddit r/diabetes: Active community with daily discussions, success stories, and peer support
  • Local support groups: Ask your hospital's diabetes educator about in-person meetings in your city
Consider sharing your own story. It might be exactly what someone newly diagnosed needs to hear. Every story of successful management gives hope to another person.
